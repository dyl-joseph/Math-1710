# 3.4 Limits at Infinity and Horizontal Asymptotes

> [!info] Section 3.4 — Limits at Infinity, Horizontal Asymptotes
> Covered on Mar 2. Stewart Calculus 9e, Section 3.4.

## Overview

Limits at infinity describe end behavior — what happens to $f(x)$ as $x \to \pm\infty$. Horizontal asymptotes are the graphical consequence. See [[Limits at Infinity]] for techniques and more examples.

## Key Definitions

> [!def] Horizontal Asymptote
> $y = L$ is a horizontal asymptote of $f$ if $\lim_{x \to \infty} f(x) = L$ or $\lim_{x \to -\infty} f(x) = L$.

> [!tip] Contrast with Vertical Asymptotes
> - **Vertical asymptote** ($x = a$): $f(x) \to \pm\infty$ as $x \to a$ (from [[Infinite Limits and Vertical Asymptotes]])
> - **Horizontal asymptote** ($y = L$): $f(x) \to L$ as $x \to \pm\infty$
> - A function can **cross** a horizontal asymptote. It can **never** cross a vertical asymptote.

## Rational Functions — The Shortcut

For $\frac{a_nx^n + \cdots}{b_mx^m + \cdots}$:

$$\lim_{x \to \pm\infty} = \begin{cases} 0 & n < m \\ \frac{a_n}{b_m} & n = m \\ \pm\infty & n > m \end{cases}$$

> [!example] Example 1
> $f(x) = \frac{2x^2 - 3}{7x^2 + 4x}$. Degrees equal, so HA: $y = \frac{2}{7}$.

> [!example] Example 2
> $f(x) = \frac{x}{x^2 + 1}$. Degree top < bottom, so HA: $y = 0$.

> [!example] Example 3
> $f(x) = \frac{x^3}{x + 2}$. Degree top > bottom, so no HA. $\lim_{x \to \infty} = \infty$.

## Worked Examples

> [!example] Example 4 — Square Root
> $$\lim_{x \to \infty} \left(\sqrt{x^2 + x} - x\right)$$
>
> Multiply by conjugate:
> $$= \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{(\sqrt{x^2+x} - x)(\sqrt{x^2+x} + x)}{\sqrt{x^2+x} + x} = \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{x^2 + x - x^2}{\sqrt{x^2+x} + x} = \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{x}{\sqrt{x^2+x} + x}$$
>
> Divide by $x$:
> $$= \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{1}{\sqrt{1 + \frac{1}{x}} + 1} = \frac{1}{1 + 1} = \frac{1}{2}$$

> [!example] Example 5 — Exponential
> Find the horizontal asymptotes of $f(x) = \frac{e^x}{1 + 2e^x}$.
>
> As $x \to \infty$: divide by $e^x$: $\frac{1}{e^{-x} + 2} \to \frac{1}{2}$. HA: $y = \frac{1}{2}$.
>
> As $x \to -\infty$: $e^x \to 0$: $\frac{0}{1 + 0} = 0$. HA: $y = 0$.

> [!example] Example 6 — End Behavior of Polynomials
> $f(x) = -2x^4 + 5x^3 - x + 3$
>
> Leading term dominates: $\lim_{x \to \pm\infty} f(x) = \lim_{x \to \pm\infty} (-2x^4) = -\infty$
>
> Both ends go down (even degree, negative leading coefficient).

## End Behavior Summary for Polynomials

| Leading term $a_nx^n$ | $x \to \infty$ | $x \to -\infty$ |
|---|---|---|
| $a_n > 0$, $n$ even | $+\infty$ | $+\infty$ |
| $a_n > 0$, $n$ odd | $+\infty$ | $-\infty$ |
| $a_n < 0$, $n$ even | $-\infty$ | $-\infty$ |
| $a_n < 0$, $n$ odd | $-\infty$ | $+\infty$ |

## Tips

> [!tip] Key Points
> - **Divide by highest power in denominator** — the standard technique for rational functions.
> - **Square roots at $-\infty$:** $\sqrt{x^2} = |x| = -x$ when $x < 0$. This is the most common mistake.
> - **Exponentials dominate polynomials:** $\lim_{x \to \infty}\frac{x^n}{e^x} = 0$ for any $n$.
> - **A function can cross its HA.** Example: $\frac{\sin x}{x}$ crosses $y = 0$ infinitely often but has HA $y = 0$.
> - **Conjugate trick** for $\sqrt{\cdots} - \text{something}$ forms (Example 4).
