# 4.3 The Fundamental Theorem of Calculus

> [!info] Section 4.3 ‚Äî The Fundamental Theorem of Calculus
> Covered on Apr 7. Stewart Calculus 9e, Section 4.3.

## Overview

The Fundamental Theorem of Calculus (FTC) connects the two branches of calculus ‚Äî differentiation and integration. It has two parts: Part 1 says differentiation undoes integration, and Part 2 gives us a practical method to evaluate definite integrals.

## FTC Part 1

> [!thm] Fundamental Theorem of Calculus, Part 1
> If $f$ is continuous on $[a, b]$, then the function
> $$g(x) = \int_a^x f(t)\,dt$$
> is continuous on $[a, b]$, differentiable on $(a, b)$, and
> $$g'(x) = f(x)$$

In other words: **the derivative of an integral (with variable upper limit) is the integrand.**

$$\frac{d}{dx}\int_a^x f(t)\,dt = f(x)$$

> [!example] Example 1 ‚Äî FTC Part 1
> Find $\frac{d}{dx}\int_1^x \sqrt{t^3 + 1}\,dt$.
>
> By FTC1: $= \sqrt{x^3 + 1}$. Done.

> [!example] Example 2 ‚Äî Chain Rule with FTC1
> Find $\frac{d}{dx}\int_0^{x^2} \sin(t)\,dt$.
>
> Let $u = x^2$. By FTC1 + chain rule:
> $$\frac{d}{dx}\int_0^{u}\sin(t)\,dt = \sin(u) \cdot \frac{du}{dx} = \sin(x^2) \cdot 2x = 2x\sin(x^2)$$

> [!example] Example 3 ‚Äî Variable in Lower Limit
> Find $\frac{d}{dx}\int_x^5 e^{t^2}\,dt$.
>
> Flip limits: $= \frac{d}{dx}\left[-\int_5^x e^{t^2}\,dt\right] = -e^{x^2}$

## FTC Part 2 (The Evaluation Theorem)

> [!thm] Fundamental Theorem of Calculus, Part 2
> If $f$ is continuous on $[a, b]$ and $F$ is any antiderivative of $f$ (i.e., $F' = f$), then
> $$\int_a^b f(x)\,dx = F(b) - F(a)$$

**Notation:** $F(x)\Big|_a^b = F(b) - F(a)$

This is the theorem that makes computing definite integrals practical ‚Äî no more limits of Riemann sums!

## Worked Examples

> [!example] Example 4 ‚Äî Polynomial
> $$\int_1^3 (x^2 - 4x + 6)\,dx = \left[\frac{x^3}{3} - 2x^2 + 6x\right]_1^3$$
> $$= \left(9 - 18 + 18\right) - \left(\frac{1}{3} - 2 + 6\right) = 9 - \frac{13}{3} = \frac{14}{3}$$

> [!example] Example 5 ‚Äî Trig
> $$\int_0^{\pi} \sin x\,dx = [-\cos x]_0^{\pi} = (-\cos\pi) - (-\cos 0) = 1 + 1 = 2$$

> [!example] Example 6 ‚Äî With $e^x$
> $$\int_0^1 e^x\,dx = [e^x]_0^1 = e - 1$$

> [!example] Example 7 ‚Äî Power Rule
> $$\int_1^4 \sqrt{x}\,dx = \int_1^4 x^{1/2}\,dx = \left[\frac{2}{3}x^{3/2}\right]_1^4 = \frac{2}{3}(8) - \frac{2}{3}(1) = \frac{16}{3} - \frac{2}{3} = \frac{14}{3}$$

> [!example] Example 8 ‚Äî Absolute Value (Split Integral)
> $$\int_0^3 |2x - 4|\,dx$$
>
> $2x - 4 = 0$ at $x = 2$. Split:
> $$= \int_0^2 (4 - 2x)\,dx + \int_2^3 (2x - 4)\,dx$$
> $$= [4x - x^2]_0^2 + [x^2 - 4x]_2^3 = (8 - 4) + [(9 - 12) - (4 - 8)]$$
> $$= 4 + [-3 + 4] = 4 + 1 = 5$$

## Summary: The Two Parts

| Part | Statement | Use |
|---|---|---|
| FTC1 | $\frac{d}{dx}\int_a^x f(t)\,dt = f(x)$ | Differentiate an integral |
| FTC2 | $\int_a^b f(x)\,dx = F(b) - F(a)$ | Evaluate a definite integral |

## Tips

> [!tip] Key Points
> - **FTC2: no "+ C" needed.** The constant cancels: $(F(b) + C) - (F(a) + C) = F(b) - F(a)$.
> - **FTC1 with chain rule:** If the upper limit is $u(x)$ instead of $x$, multiply by $u'(x)$.
> - **Variable in lower limit:** flip the sign and apply FTC1.
> - **Absolute value integrals:** find where the expression inside changes sign, split the integral, and remove the absolute value with appropriate signs.
> - **This is the most important theorem in Calculus I.**
