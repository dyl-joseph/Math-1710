# 3.1 Maximum and Minimum Values

> [!info] Section 3.1 â€” Maximum and Minimum Values
> Covered on Feb 17 and Feb 19. Stewart Calculus 9e, Section 3.1.

## Overview

This section defines what it means for a function to have maximum and minimum values, and develops a method for finding them. The key tools are the **Extreme Value Theorem**, **Fermat's Theorem**, and the **Closed Interval Method**.

## Definitions

> [!def] Absolute (Global) Extrema
> - $f$ has an **absolute maximum** at $c$ if $f(c) \ge f(x)$ for all $x$ in the domain
> - $f$ has an **absolute minimum** at $c$ if $f(c) \le f(x)$ for all $x$ in the domain
> - Together, these are called **absolute extrema** (or **global extrema**)

> [!def] Local (Relative) Extrema
> - $f$ has a **local maximum** at $c$ if $f(c) \ge f(x)$ for all $x$ *near* $c$ (in some open interval containing $c$)
> - $f$ has a **local minimum** at $c$ if $f(c) \le f(x)$ for all $x$ *near* $c$

> [!tip] Absolute vs Local
> - An absolute extremum is automatically a local extremum (unless it occurs at an endpoint)
> - A local extremum is NOT necessarily an absolute extremum
> - A function can have many local extrema but at most one absolute max and one absolute min value (though the value can be attained at multiple points)

## The Extreme Value Theorem

> [!thm] Extreme Value Theorem
> If $f$ is **continuous** on a **closed interval** $[a, b]$, then $f$ attains both an absolute maximum and an absolute minimum on $[a, b]$.

Both hypotheses are essential â€” see [[Extreme Value Theorem and Fermats Theorem]] for counterexamples.

## Fermat's Theorem

> [!thm] Fermat's Theorem
> If $f$ has a local max or min at $c$, and $f'(c)$ exists, then $f'(c) = 0$.

This means local extrema can only occur at **critical numbers**. See [[Extreme Value Theorem and Fermats Theorem]] for the proof.

## Critical Numbers

> [!def] Critical Number
> A number $c$ in the domain of $f$ is a **critical number** if $f'(c) = 0$ or $f'(c)$ does not exist.

See [[Critical Numbers]] for detailed examples and procedures.

> [!example] Example 1 â€” Finding Critical Numbers
> $f(x) = 3x^4 - 4x^3 - 12x^2 + 5$
>
> $f'(x) = 12x^3 - 12x^2 - 24x = 12x(x^2 - x - 2) = 12x(x-2)(x+1)$
>
> $f'(x) = 0$: $x = -1, 0, 2$. These are the critical numbers.

> [!example] Example 2 â€” Critical Number Where Derivative DNE
> $f(x) = x^{1/3}$
>
> $f'(x) = \frac{1}{3}x^{-2/3} = \frac{1}{3x^{2/3}}$
>
> $f'(x) \neq 0$ for any $x$. But $f'(0)$ is undefined, and $f(0) = 0$ exists.
>
> Critical number: $x = 0$.

## The Closed Interval Method

> [!def] Finding Absolute Extrema on $[a, b]$
> For $f$ continuous on $[a, b]$:
> 1. Find all critical numbers of $f$ in the **open** interval $(a, b)$
> 2. Evaluate $f$ at each critical number
> 3. Evaluate $f$ at the endpoints $a$ and $b$
> 4. **Largest** value = absolute max, **smallest** value = absolute min

> [!example] Example 3 â€” Closed Interval Method
> Find the absolute extrema of $f(x) = x^3 - 6x^2 + 9x + 2$ on $[0, 4]$.
>
> $f'(x) = 3x^2 - 12x + 9 = 3(x-1)(x-3)$
>
> Critical numbers in $(0, 4)$: $x = 1, 3$.
>
> | $x$ | $f(x)$ |
> |---|---|
> | $0$ | $2$ |
> | $1$ | $1 - 6 + 9 + 2 = 6$ |
> | $3$ | $27 - 54 + 27 + 2 = 2$ |
> | $4$ | $64 - 96 + 36 + 2 = 6$ |
>
> **Absolute max:** $6$ (at $x = 1$ and $x = 4$). **Absolute min:** $2$ (at $x = 0$ and $x = 3$).

> [!example] Example 4 â€” With Fractional Exponents
> Find absolute extrema of $f(x) = x^{2/3}$ on $[-1, 8]$.
>
> $f'(x) = \frac{2}{3}x^{-1/3}$. Never zero. Undefined at $x = 0$.
>
> Critical number in $(-1, 8)$: $x = 0$.
>
> | $x$ | $f(x)$ |
> |---|---|
> | $-1$ | $1$ |
> | $0$ | $0$ |
> | $8$ | $4$ |
>
> **Absolute max:** $4$ at $x = 8$. **Absolute min:** $0$ at $x = 0$.

> [!example] Example 5 â€” Trig Function
> Find absolute extrema of $f(x) = 2\sin x - \cos 2x$ on $[0, 2\pi]$.
>
> $f'(x) = 2\cos x + 2\sin 2x = 2\cos x + 4\sin x\cos x = 2\cos x(1 + 2\sin x)$
>
> $f'(x) = 0$: $\cos x = 0$ or $\sin x = -\frac{1}{2}$
>
> $\cos x = 0$: $x = \frac{\pi}{2}, \frac{3\pi}{2}$
>
> $\sin x = -\frac{1}{2}$: $x = \frac{7\pi}{6}, \frac{11\pi}{6}$
>
> | $x$ | $f(x)$ |
> |---|---|
> | $0$ | $0 - 1 = -1$ |
> | $\frac{\pi}{2}$ | $2 - (-1) = 3$ |
> | $\frac{7\pi}{6}$ | $-1 - \frac{1}{2} = -\frac{3}{2}$ |
> | $\frac{3\pi}{2}$ | $-2 - (-1) = -1$ |
> | $\frac{11\pi}{6}$ | $-1 - \frac{1}{2} = -\frac{3}{2}$ |
> | $2\pi$ | $0 - 1 = -1$ |
>
> **Absolute max:** $3$ at $x = \frac{\pi}{2}$. **Absolute min:** $-\frac{3}{2}$ at $x = \frac{7\pi}{6}$ and $\frac{11\pi}{6}$.

## Tips

> [!tip] Key Points
> - The Closed Interval Method **only works on closed intervals** for continuous functions.
> - **Don't forget the endpoints** â€” they are often where the absolute extrema occur.
> - **Critical number â‰  extremum.** Not every critical number gives a max or min ($f(x) = x^3$ at $x = 0$).
> - On an **open interval** or all of $\mathbb{R}$, absolute extrema may not exist. You need other methods (first/second derivative tests â€” Section 3.3).
> - When $f'$ has fractional exponents, be careful about where $f'$ is undefined.
