# 2.1 Derivatives and Rates of Change

The **derivative** is the central concept of differential calculus. It arises from two classical problems ŌĆö finding the slope of a tangent line and finding the rate at which a quantity changes ŌĆö and both turn out to have the same mathematical answer: a particular limit of a difference quotient.

---

## The Tangent Line Problem

Given a curve $y = f(x)$ and a point $P = (a, f(a))$ on it, we want the slope of the line that just "touches" the curve at $P$.

Pick a nearby point $Q = (x, f(x))$ on the curve. The **secant line** $PQ$ has slope

$$m_{\text{sec}} = \frac{f(x) - f(a)}{x - a}.$$

As $Q$ slides along the curve toward $P$ (i.e., $x \to a$), the secant line approaches a limiting position ŌĆö the **tangent line**. Its slope is

$$m = \lim_{x \to a} \frac{f(x) - f(a)}{x - a}.$$

Writing $x = a + h$ so that $h \to 0$, this becomes

$$m = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(a+h) - f(a)}{h}.$$

See [[Tangent Lines and Secant Lines]] for full details, examples, and the normal-line construction.

---

## The Derivative at a Point

> [!def] Derivative at a Point
> The **derivative of $f$ at $a$**, denoted $f'(a)$, is
> $$f'(a) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(a+h) - f(a)}{h}$$
> provided this limit exists. Equivalently,
> $$f'(a) = \lim_{x \to a} \frac{f(x) - f(a)}{x - a}.$$

The first is the **$h$-form** and the second is the **$x$-form**. Both define the same number. Use whichever form makes the algebra cleaner.

> [!tip] When Does the Derivative Fail to Exist?
> $f'(a)$ does not exist when:
> - $f$ is not continuous at $a$ (a jump, hole, or vertical asymptote),
> - the graph has a **corner** or **cusp** at $a$,
> - the tangent line is **vertical** at $a$ (slope $\to \pm\infty$).

---

## Rates of Change

> [!def] Average Rate of Change
> The **average rate of change** of $f$ over the interval $[a, b]$ is
> $$\frac{\Delta y}{\Delta x} = \frac{f(b) - f(a)}{b - a}.$$
> Geometrically this is the slope of the **secant line** through $(a, f(a))$ and $(b, f(b))$.

> [!def] Instantaneous Rate of Change
> The **instantaneous rate of change** of $f$ at $x = a$ is
> $$\lim_{\Delta x \to 0} \frac{\Delta y}{\Delta x} = f'(a).$$
> Geometrically this is the slope of the **tangent line** at $(a, f(a))$.

The instantaneous rate is the limit of average rates as the interval shrinks to zero. See [[Average vs Instantaneous Rate of Change]] for real-world interpretations, units, and additional worked examples.

---

## Velocity as a Derivative

> [!def] Instantaneous Velocity
> If $s(t)$ is the position of an object at time $t$, then the **instantaneous velocity** at time $t = a$ is
> $$v(a) = s'(a) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{s(a+h) - s(a)}{h}.$$

- The **average velocity** over $[a, a+h]$ is $\dfrac{s(a+h)-s(a)}{h}$ (a difference quotient).
- **Speed** is $|v(a)|$ ŌĆö velocity without the sign.
- A positive velocity means the object moves in the positive direction; negative means the opposite direction.

## Worked Examples

> [!example] Example 1 ŌĆö Derivative of $f(x) = x^2$ at $x = 3$
> Using the $h$-form:
> $$f'(3) = \lim_{h\to 0}\frac{(3+h)^2 - 9}{h} = \lim_{h\to 0}\frac{9+6h+h^2-9}{h} = \lim_{h\to 0}\frac{6h+h^2}{h}$$
> $$= \lim_{h\to 0}(6+h) = 6$$
>
> So $f'(3) = 6$. The tangent line at $(3,9)$ has slope $6$:
> $$y - 9 = 6(x - 3) \implies y = 6x - 9.$$

> [!example] Example 2 ŌĆö Tangent Line to $f(x) = \sqrt{x}$ at $x = 4$
> **Point:** $(4, 2)$.
>
> **Slope (h-form):**
> $$f'(4) = \lim_{h\to 0}\frac{\sqrt{4+h}-2}{h}$$
> Rationalize the numerator by multiplying by $\dfrac{\sqrt{4+h}+2}{\sqrt{4+h}+2}$:
> $$= \lim_{h\to 0}\frac{(4+h)-4}{h(\sqrt{4+h}+2)} = \lim_{h\to 0}\frac{1}{\sqrt{4+h}+2} = \frac{1}{4}$$
>
> **Tangent line:**
> $$y - 2 = \tfrac{1}{4}(x - 4) \implies y = \tfrac{1}{4}x + 1$$

> [!example] Example 3 ŌĆö Velocity from a Position Function
> A particle moves along a line with position $s(t) = t^3 - 6t$ (meters, seconds).
>
> **(a)** Average velocity on $[1, 3]$:
> $$v_{\text{avg}} = \frac{s(3)-s(1)}{3-1} = \frac{(27-18)-(1-6)}{2} = \frac{9-(-5)}{2} = \frac{14}{2} = 7\;\text{m/s}$$
>
> **(b)** Instantaneous velocity at $t = 1$:
> $$v(1) = \lim_{h\to 0}\frac{s(1+h)-s(1)}{h} = \lim_{h\to 0}\frac{[(1+h)^3 - 6(1+h)]-(-5)}{h}$$
> Expand $(1+h)^3 = 1+3h+3h^2+h^3$:
> $$= \lim_{h\to 0}\frac{1+3h+3h^2+h^3-6-6h+5}{h} = \lim_{h\to 0}\frac{-3h+3h^2+h^3}{h}$$
> $$= \lim_{h\to 0}(-3+3h+h^2) = -3\;\text{m/s}$$
>
> At $t = 1$ the particle moves in the negative direction at $3$ m/s.

> [!example] Example 4 ŌĆö Estimating a Derivative from a Table
> The temperature $T(t)$ (in ┬░C) of a cooling cup of coffee is measured:
>
> | $t$ (min) | 0 | 5 | 10 | 15 | 20 |
> |---|---|---|---|---|---|
> | $T(t)$ | 90 | 72 | 60 | 52 | 47 |
>
> Estimate $T'(10)$, the rate of cooling at $t = 10$ min.
>
> **Left difference quotient:** $\dfrac{T(10)-T(5)}{10-5} = \dfrac{60-72}{5} = -2.4$ ┬░C/min
>
> **Right difference quotient:** $\dfrac{T(15)-T(10)}{15-10} = \dfrac{52-60}{5} = -1.6$ ┬░C/min
>
> **Best estimate (symmetric):** $T'(10) \approx \dfrac{-2.4 + (-1.6)}{2} = -2.0$ ┬░C/min
>
> Equivalently: $\dfrac{T(15)-T(5)}{15-5} = \dfrac{52-72}{10} = -2.0$ ┬░C/min.
>
> The coffee is cooling at about $2$ ┬░C per minute at the $10$-minute mark.

> [!example] Example 5 ŌĆö Derivative of a Rational Function
> Find $f'(2)$ for $f(x) = \dfrac{1}{x-1}$.
>
> **$x$-form:**
> $$f'(2) = \lim_{x\to 2}\frac{\frac{1}{x-1} - 1}{x-2} = \lim_{x\to 2}\frac{\frac{1-(x-1)}{x-1}}{x-2} = \lim_{x\to 2}\frac{\frac{2-x}{x-1}}{x-2}$$
> $$= \lim_{x\to 2}\frac{-(x-2)}{(x-1)(x-2)} = \lim_{x\to 2}\frac{-1}{x-1} = -1$$
>
> So the tangent to $y = \dfrac{1}{x-1}$ at $(2,1)$ is:
> $$y - 1 = -1(x - 2) \implies y = -x + 3.$$

## Tips and Common Mistakes

> [!tip] Choosing Between the $h$-form and $x$-form
> - The **$h$-form** is usually better for polynomials and roots ŌĆö you expand $f(a+h)$ and simplify.
> - The **$x$-form** is often better for rational functions ŌĆö you can combine fractions and cancel $x - a$ directly.

> [!tip] Algebra Pitfalls
> - **Don't plug in $h = 0$ too early.** You must cancel the $h$ in the denominator first.
> - **Rationalize the numerator** when square roots appear. Multiply numerator and denominator by the conjugate.
> - **Combine fractions** before cancelling when $f$ is a rational function.
> - **Show the limit notation** throughout your work. Writing $\lim_{h\to 0}$ is not optional ŌĆö without it, the expression is just the average rate of change.

> [!tip] Interpreting the Sign
> - $f'(a) > 0$: $f$ is increasing at $a$.
> - $f'(a) < 0$: $f$ is decreasing at $a$.
> - $f'(a) = 0$: $f$ has a horizontal tangent at $a$ (possible max, min, or inflection point).
