# 5.4 Work

> [!info] Section 5.4 — Work
> Covered on Apr 20. Stewart Calculus 9e, Section 5.4.

## Overview

Work is the total effort required to perform a task — moving an object, pumping water, stretching a spring. When force is constant, $W = Fd$. When force varies, we need integration.

## Constant Force

> [!def] Work (Constant Force)
> If a constant force $F$ moves an object a distance $d$:
> $$W = Fd$$
> Units: force × distance = joules (N·m) or foot-pounds (ft·lb).

## Variable Force

> [!thm] Work (Variable Force)
> If a variable force $f(x)$ acts on an object as it moves from $x = a$ to $x = b$:
> $$W = \int_a^b f(x)\,dx$$

This comes from the Riemann sum: break the motion into small intervals where force is approximately constant.

## Application 1: Springs (Hooke's Law)

> [!def] Hooke's Law
> The force required to maintain a spring stretched (or compressed) $x$ units beyond its natural length is
> $$f(x) = kx$$
> where $k$ is the **spring constant** (units: N/m or lb/ft).

> [!example] Example 1 — Stretching a Spring
> A spring has natural length 20 cm. A force of 25 N is needed to stretch it to 30 cm. How much work to stretch it from 20 cm to 35 cm?
>
> First find $k$: $25 = k(0.10)$, so $k = 250$ N/m.
>
> Stretch from natural length: $x$ goes from $0$ to $0.15$ m.
>
> $$W = \int_0^{0.15} 250x\,dx = 250\left[\frac{x^2}{2}\right]_0^{0.15} = 125(0.0225) = 2.8125 \text{ J}$$

> [!example] Example 2 — Spring from Non-Zero Start
> Same spring. Work to stretch from 25 cm to 35 cm (i.e., from $x = 0.05$ to $x = 0.15$).
>
> $$W = \int_{0.05}^{0.15} 250x\,dx = 125[x^2]_{0.05}^{0.15} = 125(0.0225 - 0.0025) = 125(0.02) = 2.5 \text{ J}$$

## Application 2: Lifting / Rope and Chain Problems

When lifting an object whose weight changes (like a rope being coiled up), set up a variable force.

> [!example] Example 3 — Lifting a Chain
> A 50-ft chain weighing 2 lb/ft hangs from the top of a building. How much work to pull it all up?
>
> When $x$ ft of chain has been pulled up, the remaining length is $(50 - x)$ ft, weighing $2(50 - x)$ lb.
>
> $$W = \int_0^{50} 2(50 - x)\,dx = 2\left[50x - \frac{x^2}{2}\right]_0^{50} = 2(2500 - 1250) = 2500 \text{ ft·lb}$$

> [!example] Example 4 — Lifting a Bucket
> A 40-lb bucket is lifted 80 ft by a rope weighing 0.5 lb/ft. Find total work.
>
> **Bucket:** $W_{\text{bucket}} = 40 \times 80 = 3200$ ft·lb (constant force).
>
> **Rope:** When $x$ ft has been pulled up, remaining rope weighs $0.5(80-x)$ lb.
> $$W_{\text{rope}} = \int_0^{80} 0.5(80-x)\,dx = 0.5\left[80x - \frac{x^2}{2}\right]_0^{80} = 0.5(3200) = 1600 \text{ ft·lb}$$
>
> **Total:** $3200 + 1600 = 4800$ ft·lb.

## Application 3: Pumping Water

> [!def] Setup for Pumping Problems
> 1. Set up a coordinate system (usually $y$-axis pointing up)
> 2. Consider a thin horizontal slice of water at height $y$ with thickness $dy$
> 3. Find the **volume** of the slice: $dV = A(y)\,dy$ where $A(y)$ is the cross-sectional area
> 4. Find the **weight** of the slice: $dF = \rho g \cdot A(y)\,dy$ (or $\rho \cdot A(y)\,dy$ in imperial)
> 5. Find the **distance** the slice must be lifted: $d(y)$
> 6. Integrate: $W = \int \rho g \cdot A(y) \cdot d(y)\,dy$

Water density: $\rho = 1000$ kg/m³, $g = 9.8$ m/s². Or: water weighs $62.5$ lb/ft³.

> [!example] Example 5 — Pumping from a Cylindrical Tank
> A cylindrical tank has radius 3 m and height 5 m, filled with water. Pump all water over the top.
>
> Place origin at base. Slice at height $y$: $A(y) = \pi(3)^2 = 9\pi$. Distance to top: $5 - y$.
>
> $$W = \int_0^5 9800 \cdot 9\pi \cdot (5-y)\,dy = 88200\pi\int_0^5(5-y)\,dy$$
> $$= 88200\pi\left[5y - \frac{y^2}{2}\right]_0^5 = 88200\pi \cdot \frac{25}{2} = 1{,}102{,}500\pi \approx 3{,}463{,}606 \text{ J}$$

> [!example] Example 6 — Pumping from a Conical Tank
> An inverted cone (vertex at bottom) has height 6 m and top radius 3 m. Full of water. Pump water to a point 2 m above the top.
>
> Place origin at vertex. At height $y$: by similar triangles, $r(y) = \frac{3}{6}y = \frac{y}{2}$.
>
> $A(y) = \pi\left(\frac{y}{2}\right)^2 = \frac{\pi y^2}{4}$
>
> Distance to discharge point (2 m above top, which is at $y = 6$): $d(y) = 8 - y$.
>
> $$W = \int_0^6 9800 \cdot \frac{\pi y^2}{4} \cdot (8-y)\,dy = 2450\pi\int_0^6(8y^2 - y^3)\,dy$$
> $$= 2450\pi\left[\frac{8y^3}{3} - \frac{y^4}{4}\right]_0^6 = 2450\pi\left(576 - 324\right) = 2450\pi(252) = 617{,}400\pi \text{ J}$$

## Tips

> [!tip] Key Points
> - **Always identify the variable:** what changes as work is done? That's your integration variable.
> - **Springs:** measure $x$ from the natural length. The limits are displacements from natural, not total lengths.
> - **Pumping:** set up coordinates clearly. The distance each slice travels depends on where it starts and where it's going.
> - **Units matter:** be consistent. SI (newtons, meters, joules) or imperial (pounds, feet, foot-pounds).
> - **Weight vs mass:** in imperial, weight = force directly (lb). In SI, force = mass × $g$ = $\rho V g$.
