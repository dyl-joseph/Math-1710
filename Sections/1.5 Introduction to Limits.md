# 1.5 Introduction to Limits

## Why Limits Matter

The **limit** is the foundational concept of calculus. Nearly everything in this course depends on it:

- **Derivatives** are defined as limits: $f'(x) = \displaystyle\lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(x+h) - f(x)}{h}$
- **Integrals** are defined as limits of Riemann sums
- **Continuity** means $\lim_{x \to a} f(x) = f(a)$
- Series, improper integrals, and more all rely on limits

This section develops the **intuitive** idea of a limit. The precise $\varepsilon$-$\delta$ definition comes in Section 1.7.

## Informal Definition

> [!def] Informal Definition of a Limit
> We write
> $$\lim_{x \to a} f(x) = L$$
> and say "the limit of $f(x)$ as $x$ approaches $a$ equals $L$" if we can make the values of $f(x)$ **arbitrarily close** to $L$ by taking $x$ **sufficiently close** to $a$ (on either side of $a$) **but not equal to** $a$.

> [!tip] The Single Most Important Thing About Limits
> **The limit asks what $f(x)$ is *approaching*, not what $f(x)$ *equals*.** The value $f(a)$ — or whether $f(a)$ even exists — is completely irrelevant to $\lim_{x \to a} f(x)$.

For a deeper discussion of what this definition means and common misconceptions, see [[Intuitive Idea of a Limit]].

## Estimating Limits from Tables

We can estimate a limit by computing $f(x)$ for values of $x$ that approach $a$ from both sides.

> [!example] Example 1 — Table Estimation
> Estimate $\displaystyle\lim_{x \to 0} \frac{\sqrt{x+1} - 1}{x}$.
>
> This is a $\frac{0}{0}$ indeterminate form at $x = 0$. Compute values near $0$:
>
> | $x$ | $f(x)$ |
> |---|---|
> | $-0.1$ | $0.51317$ |
> | $-0.01$ | $0.50126$ |
> | $-0.001$ | $0.50013$ |
> | $0.001$ | $0.49988$ |
> | $0.01$ | $0.49876$ |
> | $0.1$ | $0.48810$ |
>
> From both sides, $f(x)$ approaches $0.5$. So we estimate:
> $$\lim_{x \to 0} \frac{\sqrt{x+1} - 1}{x} = \frac{1}{2}$$
>
> (This can be confirmed algebraically by multiplying by the conjugate $\frac{\sqrt{x+1}+1}{\sqrt{x+1}+1}$.)

> [!tip] Pitfall: Tables Can Deceive
> Tables only show finitely many values. A function could oscillate or behave erratically between the values you check. Always treat table estimates as **evidence**, not proof.

## Estimating Limits from Graphs

On a graph, $\lim_{x \to a} f(x) = L$ means: as you trace the curve toward $x = a$ from both sides, the $y$-values approach $L$.

> [!example] Example 2 — Graphical Estimation
> Consider the graph of a function $f$ where:
> - As $x$ approaches $2$ from the left, the curve rises toward $y = 4$.
> - As $x$ approaches $2$ from the right, the curve also approaches $y = 4$.
> - There is an open circle at $(2, 4)$ and a solid dot at $(2, 1)$ — so $f(2) = 1$.
>
> Reading the graph:
> $$\lim_{x \to 2} f(x) = 4$$
>
> Even though $f(2) = 1$, the limit is $4$ because the limit depends on the **approaching behavior**, not the function value.

Key graphical clues:
- **Open circle** at $(a, L)$: the function is not defined (or has a different value) at $a$, but the limit is $L$.
- **Solid dot** at $(a, L)$ with the curve passing through: $f(a) = L$ and likely $\lim_{x \to a} f(x) = L$.
- **Jump**: the curve approaches different heights from left and right — two-sided limit DNE.

## One-Sided Limits

Sometimes we need to consider limits from just one direction.

> [!def] One-Sided Limits (Preview)
> $$\lim_{x \to a^-} f(x) = L \quad \text{(left-hand limit: } x \text{ approaches } a \text{ from below)}$$
> $$\lim_{x \to a^+} f(x) = L \quad \text{(right-hand limit: } x \text{ approaches } a \text{ from above)}$$

> [!thm] Connection to the Two-Sided Limit
> $$\lim_{x \to a} f(x) = L \quad \Longleftrightarrow \quad \lim_{x \to a^-} f(x) = L \;\;\text{and}\;\; \lim_{x \to a^+} f(x) = L$$

The two-sided limit exists if and only if both one-sided limits exist and are equal.

> [!example] Example 3 — One-Sided Limits from a Piecewise Function
> Let
> $$f(x) = \begin{cases} 3 - x & \text{if } x < 1 \\ x^2 & \text{if } x \geq 1 \end{cases}$$
>
> **Left-hand limit at $x = 1$:** Use $f(x) = 3 - x$:
> $$\lim_{x \to 1^-} f(x) = 3 - 1 = 2$$
>
> **Right-hand limit at $x = 1$:** Use $f(x) = x^2$:
> $$\lim_{x \to 1^+} f(x) = 1^2 = 1$$
>
> Since $2 \neq 1$, $\lim_{x \to 1} f(x)$ **does not exist**.

For more detail: [[One-Sided Limits]]

## Infinite Limits and Vertical Asymptotes

When a function "blows up" near a point, we use infinite limit notation.

> [!def] Vertical Asymptote (Preview)
> The line $x = a$ is a **vertical asymptote** of $y = f(x)$ if $f(x) \to \pm\infty$ as $x \to a$ (from at least one side).

> [!example] Example 4 — Infinite Limit
> Find $\displaystyle\lim_{x \to 3} \frac{1}{(x-3)^2}$.
>
> As $x \to 3$, $(x - 3)^2 \to 0^+$ (always positive since it's squared). Therefore:
> $$\frac{1}{(x-3)^2} \to +\infty$$
>
> We write $\displaystyle\lim_{x \to 3} \frac{1}{(x-3)^2} = \infty$. The line $x = 3$ is a vertical asymptote.

For the full treatment including sign analysis and more examples: [[Infinite Limits and Vertical Asymptotes]]

## Common Pitfalls

> [!tip] Avoid These Mistakes
> 1. **Confusing the limit with the function value.** Always ask "what is $f(x)$ *approaching*?" not "what is $f(a)$?"
> 2. **Forgetting to check both sides.** A limit might exist from the left but not match the right — then the two-sided limit DNE.
> 3. **Trusting a single table.** A table with $x = 0.1, 0.01, 0.001$ might suggest a pattern that breaks at $x = 0.0001$. Use tables as evidence, not proof.
> 4. **Writing "$\lim = \text{DNE}$" without explanation.** State *why* the limit doesn't exist: different one-sided limits? Unbounded? Oscillation?
> 5. **Saying $\lim_{x \to a} f(x) = \infty$ means the limit "exists."** Technically, the limit does not exist — "$= \infty$" is shorthand describing *how* it fails to exist.

## Summary

| Concept | Key Idea |
|---|---|
| $\lim_{x \to a} f(x) = L$ | $f(x) \to L$ as $x \to a$, regardless of $f(a)$ |
| Table estimation | Approach $a$ from both sides; look for convergence |
| Graph estimation | Trace the curve from both sides toward $x = a$ |
| One-sided limits | Must agree for two-sided limit to exist |
| Infinite limits | $f(x) \to \pm\infty$ → vertical asymptote at $x = a$ |
| DNE causes | Different one-sided limits, unbounded, oscillation |
