# 2.4 Derivatives of Trigonometric Functions

## Overview

This section develops the derivatives of all six trigonometric functions. We start from the limit definition to prove the derivatives of $\sin x$ and $\cos x$, then use the quotient rule to derive the remaining four. The results are used constantly throughout the rest of calculus.

---

## Prerequisites: Two Key Limits

Both proofs below depend on two limits established in [[1.6 Calculating Limits|Section 1.6]] using the Squeeze Theorem and geometric arguments:

> [!thm] Key Trigonometric Limits
> $$\lim_{h \to 0} \frac{\sin h}{h} = 1 \qquad \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{\cos h - 1}{h} = 0$$

These hold when $h$ is measured in **radians** — this is why calculus always uses radians.

---

## Derivative of $\sin x$

> [!thm] Derivative of Sine
> $$\frac{d}{dx}(\sin x) = \cos x$$

**Proof.** From the limit definition:

$$\frac{d}{dx}(\sin x) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{\sin(x+h) - \sin x}{h}$$

Expand using the angle addition formula $\sin(x+h) = \sin x \cos h + \cos x \sin h$:

$$= \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{\sin x \cos h + \cos x \sin h - \sin x}{h}$$

$$= \lim_{h \to 0} \left[\sin x \cdot \frac{\cos h - 1}{h} + \cos x \cdot \frac{\sin h}{h}\right]$$

$$= \sin x \cdot 0 + \cos x \cdot 1 = \cos x \qquad \blacksquare$$

---

## Derivative of $\cos x$

> [!thm] Derivative of Cosine
> $$\frac{d}{dx}(\cos x) = -\sin x$$

**Proof.** From the limit definition:

$$\frac{d}{dx}(\cos x) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{\cos(x+h) - \cos x}{h}$$

Expand using $\cos(x+h) = \cos x \cos h - \sin x \sin h$:

$$= \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{\cos x \cos h - \sin x \sin h - \cos x}{h}$$

$$= \lim_{h \to 0} \left[\cos x \cdot \frac{\cos h - 1}{h} - \sin x \cdot \frac{\sin h}{h}\right]$$

$$= \cos x \cdot 0 - \sin x \cdot 1 = -\sin x \qquad \blacksquare$$

For expanded proofs, graphical verification, and the cyclic derivative pattern, see [[Derivatives of Sine and Cosine]].

---

## Derivatives of the Other Four Trig Functions

Each is derived from $\sin x$ and $\cos x$ using the quotient rule. Full proofs are in [[Derivatives of Other Trig Functions]].

> [!thm] Derivative of Tangent
> $$\frac{d}{dx}(\tan x) = \sec^2 x$$
>
> *Proof sketch:* Write $\tan x = \frac{\sin x}{\cos x}$, apply quotient rule, use $\sin^2 x + \cos^2 x = 1$.

> [!thm] Derivative of Cotangent
> $$\frac{d}{dx}(\cot x) = -\csc^2 x$$

> [!thm] Derivative of Secant
> $$\frac{d}{dx}(\sec x) = \sec x \tan x$$

> [!thm] Derivative of Cosecant
> $$\frac{d}{dx}(\csc x) = -\csc x \cot x$$

## Memory Aids

> [!tip] The "co-function" negative sign pattern
> - $\sin x \to \cos x$ (positive) vs. $\cos x \to -\sin x$ (negative)
> - $\tan x \to \sec^2 x$ (positive) vs. $\cot x \to -\csc^2 x$ (negative)
> - $\sec x \to \sec x \tan x$ (positive) vs. $\csc x \to -\csc x \cot x$ (negative)
>
> **Every "co-" function gets a negative sign in its derivative.** The non-co functions ($\sin$, $\tan$, $\sec$) have positive derivatives; their co-function partners ($\cos$, $\cot$, $\csc$) pick up a minus sign.

> [!tip] Structural patterns
> - The $(\tan, \sec)$ pair: both derivatives involve $\sec$.
> - The $(\cot, \csc)$ pair: both derivatives involve $\csc$ (and both are negative).
> - $\sec$ and $\csc$ derivatives are "product-like" ($\sec x \tan x$, $\csc x \cot x$), while $\tan$ and $\cot$ derivatives are "square" ($\sec^2 x$, $\csc^2 x$).

## Worked Examples

> [!example] Example 1 — Product rule + trig
> Differentiate $f(x) = x^2 \sin x$.
>
> Apply the product rule: $(uv)' = u'v + uv'$ with $u = x^2$, $v = \sin x$:
>
> $$f'(x) = 2x \sin x + x^2 \cos x$$

> [!example] Example 2 — Quotient rule + trig identity
> Differentiate $f(x) = \dfrac{\sin x}{1 + \cos x}$.
>
> Apply the quotient rule:
>
> $$f'(x) = \frac{\cos x (1 + \cos x) - \sin x (-\sin x)}{(1 + \cos x)^2}$$
>
> $$= \frac{\cos x + \cos^2 x + \sin^2 x}{(1 + \cos x)^2}$$
>
> Use $\sin^2 x + \cos^2 x = 1$:
>
> $$= \frac{\cos x + 1}{(1 + \cos x)^2} = \frac{1}{1 + \cos x}$$

> [!example] Example 3 — Product rule with sec and tan
> Differentiate $f(x) = \sec x \tan x$.
>
> Product rule with $u = \sec x$, $v = \tan x$:
>
> $$f'(x) = (\sec x \tan x)(\tan x) + (\sec x)(\sec^2 x)$$
>
> $$= \sec x \tan^2 x + \sec^3 x$$
>
> Factor out $\sec x$:
>
> $$= \sec x(\tan^2 x + \sec^2 x)$$

> [!example] Example 4 — Equation of a tangent line
> Find the equation of the tangent line to $y = \tan x$ at $x = \frac{\pi}{4}$.
>
> **Step 1 — Find the point.** At $x = \frac{\pi}{4}$:
>
> $$y = \tan\frac{\pi}{4} = 1$$
>
> So the point is $\left(\frac{\pi}{4}, 1\right)$.
>
> **Step 2 — Find the slope.** Since $\frac{d}{dx}(\tan x) = \sec^2 x$:
>
> $$m = \sec^2\frac{\pi}{4} = \left(\frac{1}{\cos(\pi/4)}\right)^2 = \left(\frac{1}{{\sqrt{2}}/{2}}\right)^2 = (\sqrt{2})^2 = 2$$
>
> **Step 3 — Point-slope form:**
>
> $$y - 1 = 2\left(x - \frac{\pi}{4}\right) \quad \Longrightarrow \quad y = 2x - \frac{\pi}{2} + 1$$

> [!example] Example 5 — Simple harmonic motion
> A particle moves along a line with position $s(t) = A\cos(\omega t)$, where $A$ is amplitude and $\omega$ is angular frequency.
>
> Find the velocity $v(t) = s'(t)$.
>
> Using the chain rule (preview from Section 2.5), with outer function $\cos$ and inner function $\omega t$:
>
> $$v(t) = s'(t) = -A\sin(\omega t) \cdot \omega = -A\omega \sin(\omega t)$$
>
> Key observations:
> - The velocity oscillates with the **same frequency** as the position.
> - Maximum speed is $|A\omega|$, occurring when $\sin(\omega t) = \pm 1$ (i.e., when the particle passes through the equilibrium position).
> - Velocity is zero when $\sin(\omega t) = 0$ (i.e., at the extreme positions where the particle reverses direction).

## Common Mistakes

> [!tip] Watch out for these errors
> - **Forgetting the negative sign** on derivatives of $\cos x$, $\cot x$, $\csc x$.
> - **Mixing up $\sec^2 x$ and $\sec x \tan x$**: $\sec^2 x$ is the derivative of $\tan x$; $\sec x \tan x$ is the derivative of $\sec x$.
> - **Using degrees instead of radians.** The formulas $\frac{d}{dx}(\sin x) = \cos x$ etc. are valid only when $x$ is in radians. In degrees, extra factors of $\frac{\pi}{180}$ appear.
> - **Forgetting product/quotient rules.** For example, $\frac{d}{dx}(x \sin x) \neq x \cos x$. You must apply the product rule.
