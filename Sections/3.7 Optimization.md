# 3.7 Optimization Problems

> [!info] Section 3.7 — Optimization Problems
> Covered on Mar 16 and Mar 17. Stewart Calculus 9e, Section 3.7.

## Overview

Optimization problems ask you to find the maximum or minimum value of a quantity subject to constraints. This is one of the most important applications of derivatives and a **major exam topic**.

See [[Optimization Problem-Solving Strategy]] for the full procedure, common constraints, and additional examples.

## The Method

1. Express the quantity to optimize as a function of **one variable**
2. Use constraints to eliminate extra variables
3. Find the absolute max or min (critical numbers + endpoint check or derivative test)

## Worked Examples

> [!example] Example 1 — Fencing with Fixed Perimeter
> A farmer has 2400 ft of fencing and wants to fence a rectangular field bordering a straight river. No fence is needed along the river. Find the dimensions that maximize the area.
>
> Let width $= w$, length along river $= l$. Constraint: $l + 2w = 2400$, so $l = 2400 - 2w$.
>
> $$A(w) = lw = (2400 - 2w)w = 2400w - 2w^2, \quad 0 < w < 1200$$
>
> $A'(w) = 2400 - 4w = 0 \implies w = 600$. Then $l = 1200$.
>
> $A''(w) = -4 < 0$ → confirmed max.
>
> **Maximum area: $1200 \times 600 = 720{,}000$ ft².**

> [!example] Example 2 — Minimum Material for a Can
> A cylindrical can must hold 1 L ($1000$ cm³). Find the dimensions that minimize the cost of material (surface area).
>
> Volume constraint: $\pi r^2 h = 1000 \implies h = \frac{1000}{\pi r^2}$
>
> Surface area: $S = 2\pi r^2 + 2\pi rh = 2\pi r^2 + 2\pi r \cdot \frac{1000}{\pi r^2} = 2\pi r^2 + \frac{2000}{r}$
>
> Domain: $r > 0$.
>
> $S'(r) = 4\pi r - \frac{2000}{r^2} = 0 \implies 4\pi r^3 = 2000 \implies r = \left(\frac{500}{\pi}\right)^{1/3} \approx 5.42$ cm
>
> $h = \frac{1000}{\pi r^2} = \frac{1000}{\pi(500/\pi)^{2/3}} = 2\left(\frac{500}{\pi}\right)^{1/3} = 2r$
>
> $S''(r) = 4\pi + \frac{4000}{r^3} > 0$ → confirmed min.
>
> **Optimal can has $h = 2r$ (height equals diameter).**

> [!example] Example 3 — Box with Maximum Volume
> An open-top box is made from a $24 \times 24$ cm sheet by cutting equal squares of side $x$ from corners and folding up. Find $x$ that maximizes volume.
>
> $V(x) = x(24 - 2x)^2, \quad 0 < x < 12$
>
> $V'(x) = (24-2x)^2 + x \cdot 2(24-2x)(-2) = (24-2x)[(24-2x) - 4x] = (24-2x)(24-6x)$
>
> $V'(x) = 0$: $x = 12$ (not in domain) or $x = 4$.
>
> $V(4) = 4(16)^2 = 1024$ cm³. Check endpoints: $V(0) = 0$, $V(12) = 0$.
>
> **Maximum volume: $1024$ cm³ with $x = 4$ cm.**

> [!example] Example 4 — Closest Point to a Line
> Find the point on the parabola $y = x^2$ that is closest to the point $(0, 1)$.
>
> Minimize $D^2 = x^2 + (x^2 - 1)^2 = x^2 + x^4 - 2x^2 + 1 = x^4 - x^2 + 1$
>
> $\frac{d}{dx}(D^2) = 4x^3 - 2x = 2x(2x^2 - 1) = 0$
>
> $x = 0$ or $x = \pm\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}$
>
> $D^2(0) = 1$, $D^2(\pm\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}) = \frac{1}{4} - \frac{1}{2} + 1 = \frac{3}{4}$
>
> Min at $x = \pm\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}$: points $\left(\pm\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}, \frac{1}{2}\right)$, distance $= \frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}$.

> [!example] Example 5 — Inscribed Rectangle
> A rectangle is inscribed under the parabola $y = 8 - x^2$ and above the $x$-axis. Maximize its area.
>
> By symmetry, corners at $(\pm x, 0)$ and $(\pm x, 8 - x^2)$.
>
> $A(x) = 2x(8 - x^2) = 16x - 2x^3, \quad 0 < x < 2\sqrt{2}$
>
> $A'(x) = 16 - 6x^2 = 0 \implies x = \frac{4}{\sqrt{6}} = \frac{4\sqrt{6}}{6} = \frac{2\sqrt{6}}{3}$
>
> $y = 8 - \frac{16}{6} = 8 - \frac{8}{3} = \frac{16}{3}$
>
> $A = 2 \cdot \frac{2\sqrt{6}}{3} \cdot \frac{16}{3} = \frac{64\sqrt{6}}{9} \approx 17.4$
>
> $A'' = -12x < 0$ → confirmed max.

> [!example] Example 6 — Minimum Distance Traveled
> A house is 1 km from a road. The nearest town on the road is 3 km down the road. Where should you hit the road to minimize total walking distance to town?
>
> Let $x$ = distance along road from the nearest point to where you hit the road.
>
> Total distance: $D(x) = \sqrt{1 + x^2} + (3 - x), \quad 0 \le x \le 3$
>
> $D'(x) = \frac{x}{\sqrt{1+x^2}} - 1 = 0 \implies \frac{x}{\sqrt{1+x^2}} = 1$
>
> $x^2 = 1 + x^2$: no solution! So $D'(x) < 0$ for all $x \in [0, 3)$.
>
> $D$ is decreasing on $[0, 3]$, so minimum is at $x = 3$: walk straight to town.
>
> $D(3) = \sqrt{10} \approx 3.16$ km.

## Tips

> [!tip] Exam Strategy
> - **Draw a picture.** Always. Even if simple.
> - **Clearly state your variables** and what you're optimizing.
> - **Show the constraint and substitution.** The grader needs to see how you reduced to one variable.
> - **Check endpoints and verify max vs min.** Use Second Derivative Test or endpoint comparison.
> - **Answer what's asked.** If they want dimensions, give all of them. If they want the maximum value, compute it.
> - **Common trick:** if optimizing distance, optimize $D^2$ instead to avoid square roots.
