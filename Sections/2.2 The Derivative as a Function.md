# 2.2 The Derivative as a Function

In Section 2.1 we defined the derivative $f'(a)$ as the slope of the tangent line at a **single** point $x=a$. Now we let $a$ vary: by replacing the fixed number $a$ with the variable $x$, the derivative becomes a **new function** $f'(x)$ whose value at any $x$ is the slope of the tangent to $y=f(x)$ at that point.

---

## The Derivative Function

> [!def] Derivative as a Function
> The **derivative of $f$** is the function $f'$ defined by
> $$f'(x) = \lim_{h\to 0}\frac{f(x+h)-f(x)}{h}$$
> for all $x$ where this limit exists. The domain of $f'$ is the set of all $x$ values at which $f$ is [[Differentiability|differentiable]].

---

## Notation

Several notations are used interchangeably for the derivative of $y=f(x)$:

| Notation | Name / Origin |
|----------|--------------|
| $f'(x)$ | **Lagrange notation** — most common in single-variable calculus |
| $\dfrac{dy}{dx}$ | **Leibniz notation** — emphasizes the derivative as a ratio of infinitesimal changes; useful in chain rule and related-rates problems |
| $\dfrac{df}{dx}$ | Leibniz form using the function name |
| $Df(x)$ | **Euler notation** — $D$ is the "differentiation operator" |
| $D_x f(x)$ | Euler notation with the variable of differentiation made explicit |

> [!tip] $\frac{dy}{dx}$ Is Not a Fraction (Yet)
> For now, treat $\frac{dy}{dx}$ as a single symbol meaning "the derivative of $y$ with respect to $x$." It is **not** a quotient of two separate quantities $dy$ and $dx$ — though the notation is deliberately suggestive, and differentials will make the fraction interpretation precise later (Section 2.9).

When we want to evaluate the derivative at a specific number $a$, we write:
$$f'(a) \quad\text{or}\quad \left.\frac{dy}{dx}\right|_{x=a}$$

---

## Computing Derivatives from the Limit Definition

### Example 1 — $f(x)=x^3$

> [!example] $f(x)=x^3$
> $$f'(x)=\lim_{h\to 0}\frac{(x+h)^3-x^3}{h}$$
>
> Expand $(x+h)^3 = x^3+3x^2h+3xh^2+h^3$:
> $$=\lim_{h\to 0}\frac{3x^2h+3xh^2+h^3}{h}=\lim_{h\to 0}(3x^2+3xh+h^2)=3x^2$$
>
> **Result:** $f'(x)=3x^2$.

### Example 2 — $f(x)=\sqrt{x}$

> [!example] $f(x)=\sqrt{x}$
> $$f'(x)=\lim_{h\to 0}\frac{\sqrt{x+h}-\sqrt{x}}{h}$$
>
> Multiply by the conjugate:
> $$=\lim_{h\to 0}\frac{(\sqrt{x+h}-\sqrt{x})(\sqrt{x+h}+\sqrt{x})}{h(\sqrt{x+h}+\sqrt{x})}=\lim_{h\to 0}\frac{(x+h)-x}{h(\sqrt{x+h}+\sqrt{x})}$$
>
> $$=\lim_{h\to 0}\frac{1}{\sqrt{x+h}+\sqrt{x}}=\frac{1}{2\sqrt{x}}$$
>
> **Result:** $f'(x)=\dfrac{1}{2\sqrt{x}}$, with domain $x>0$.

### Example 3 — $f(x)=\dfrac{1}{x}$

> [!example] $f(x)=\dfrac{1}{x}$
> $$f'(x)=\lim_{h\to 0}\frac{\frac{1}{x+h}-\frac{1}{x}}{h}=\lim_{h\to 0}\frac{\frac{x-(x+h)}{x(x+h)}}{h}$$
>
> $$=\lim_{h\to 0}\frac{-h}{h\cdot x(x+h)}=\lim_{h\to 0}\frac{-1}{x(x+h)}=\frac{-1}{x^2}$$
>
> **Result:** $f'(x)=-\dfrac{1}{x^2}$, with domain $x\neq 0$.

## Graphing $f'$ from the Graph of $f$

You can sketch $f'$ by reading slopes off the graph of $f$:

| What you see on $f$ | What it means for $f'$ |
|---------------------|----------------------|
| $f$ is **increasing** | $f'(x)>0$ (graph of $f'$ above $x$-axis) |
| $f$ is **decreasing** | $f'(x)<0$ (graph of $f'$ below $x$-axis) |
| $f$ has a **horizontal tangent** | $f'(x)=0$ ($f'$ crosses or touches the $x$-axis) |
| $f$ is **steep** | $|f'(x)|$ is large |
| $f$ is nearly **flat** | $|f'(x)|$ is close to $0$ |

> [!tip] Slope-Reading Strategy
> 1. Mark every point where $f$ has a horizontal tangent — plot these as zeros of $f'$.
> 2. Between those points, determine whether $f$ is increasing ($f'>0$) or decreasing ($f'<0$).
> 3. Estimate the steepness to judge how far $f'$ is from zero.

## Differentiability

> [!def] Differentiable at a Point
> $f$ is **differentiable at $a$** if $f'(a)$ exists — that is, the limit
> $$\lim_{h\to 0}\frac{f(a+h)-f(a)}{h}$$
> exists and is finite.

A function can fail to be differentiable at a point if there is a **corner**, a **vertical tangent**, or a **discontinuity**. See [[Differentiability]] for a full treatment with examples.

---

## Differentiable Implies Continuous

> [!thm] Differentiability Implies Continuity
> If $f$ is differentiable at $a$, then $f$ is continuous at $a$.

**Contrapositive:** If $f$ is not continuous at $a$, then $f$ is not differentiable at $a$.

> [!tip] The Converse Is False
> Continuity does **not** imply differentiability. The classic counterexample is $f(x)=|x|$: continuous everywhere, but not differentiable at $x=0$ (corner). See [[Differentiability]] for more.

## Higher-Order Derivatives

Since $f'$ is a function, we can differentiate it to get the **second derivative** $f''$, and so on:

$$f'(x),\quad f''(x),\quad f'''(x),\quad f^{(4)}(x),\;\dots$$

In Leibniz notation:

$$\frac{dy}{dx},\quad \frac{d^2y}{dx^2},\quad \frac{d^3y}{dx^3},\quad \frac{d^4y}{dx^4},\;\dots$$

See [[Higher-Order Derivatives]] for notation, physical interpretations, and examples.

### Acceleration

If $s(t)$ is the position of a particle at time $t$, then:

- $v(t)=s'(t)$ is the **velocity**
- $a(t)=v'(t)=s''(t)$ is the **acceleration**

> [!example] Position, Velocity, and Acceleration
> A particle moves along a line with position $s(t)=t^3-6t^2+9t$ (metres, $t$ in seconds).
>
> - $v(t)=s'(t)=3t^2-12t+9$
> - $a(t)=s''(t)=6t-12$
>
> At $t=2$: velocity $=3(4)-12(2)+9=-3$ m/s (moving left), acceleration $=6(2)-12=0$ m/s$^2$.

## Tips

> [!tip] Common Mistakes
> - Forgetting to take the limit — writing the difference quotient and stopping.
> - Dropping $h$-terms too early (before cancelling with the denominator $h$).
> - Confusing "not differentiable" with "derivative is zero." A horizontal tangent means $f'=0$; a corner or cusp means $f'$ **does not exist**.
