# 5.2 Volumes

> [!info] Section 5.2 — Volumes
> Covered on Apr 16. Stewart Calculus 9e, Section 5.2.

## Overview

We compute volumes of solids by slicing them into thin cross-sections, finding the area of each slice, and integrating.

## General Slicing Method

> [!thm] Volume by Cross-Sections
> If the cross-sectional area at position $x$ is $A(x)$, then the volume is
> $$V = \int_a^b A(x)\,dx$$

## Solids of Revolution — Disk Method

When a region is revolved around an axis, each cross-section is a **disk** (circle).

> [!thm] Disk Method — Revolving Around the $x$-axis
> If $y = f(x) \ge 0$ is revolved around the $x$-axis:
> $$V = \int_a^b \pi[f(x)]^2\,dx$$

> [!thm] Disk Method — Revolving Around the $y$-axis
> If $x = g(y) \ge 0$ is revolved around the $y$-axis:
> $$V = \int_c^d \pi[g(y)]^2\,dy$$

## Washer Method

When there's a hole in the middle (region between two curves revolved):

> [!thm] Washer Method
> If the region between $y = f(x)$ (outer) and $y = g(x)$ (inner) is revolved around the $x$-axis:
> $$V = \int_a^b \pi\left([f(x)]^2 - [g(x)]^2\right)\,dx$$
>
> $R = f(x)$ = outer radius, $r = g(x)$ = inner radius.

## Worked Examples

> [!example] Example 1 — Disk (Basic)
> Revolve $y = \sqrt{x}$ from $x = 0$ to $x = 4$ around the $x$-axis.
>
> $$V = \int_0^4 \pi(\sqrt{x})^2\,dx = \pi\int_0^4 x\,dx = \pi\left[\frac{x^2}{2}\right]_0^4 = 8\pi$$

> [!example] Example 2 — Disk Around $y$-axis
> Revolve $y = x^2$ ($0 \le y \le 4$) around the $y$-axis.
>
> Solve for $x$: $x = \sqrt{y}$. Integrate with respect to $y$:
>
> $$V = \int_0^4 \pi(\sqrt{y})^2\,dy = \pi\int_0^4 y\,dy = \pi\left[\frac{y^2}{2}\right]_0^4 = 8\pi$$

> [!example] Example 3 — Washer
> Revolve the region between $y = x^2$ and $y = x$ around the $x$-axis.
>
> Intersections: $x^2 = x \implies x = 0, 1$. Outer: $y = x$, inner: $y = x^2$ (on $[0,1]$).
>
> $$V = \int_0^1 \pi(x^2 - x^4)\,dx = \pi\left[\frac{x^3}{3} - \frac{x^5}{5}\right]_0^1 = \pi\left(\frac{1}{3} - \frac{1}{5}\right) = \frac{2\pi}{15}$$

> [!example] Example 4 — Revolution Around a Line
> Revolve $y = x^2$ from $x = 0$ to $x = 2$ around $y = -1$.
>
> The radius from $y = -1$ to the curve is $R(x) = x^2 - (-1) = x^2 + 1$.
>
> $$V = \int_0^2 \pi(x^2+1)^2\,dx = \pi\int_0^2(x^4 + 2x^2 + 1)\,dx$$
> $$= \pi\left[\frac{x^5}{5} + \frac{2x^3}{3} + x\right]_0^2 = \pi\left(\frac{32}{5} + \frac{16}{3} + 2\right) = \frac{256\pi}{15}$$

> [!example] Example 5 — Known Cross-Sections (Non-Revolution)
> A solid has base the region under $y = 1 - x^2$ (above $x$-axis). Cross-sections perpendicular to the $x$-axis are squares.
>
> Side of square $= f(x) = 1 - x^2$. Base extends from $x = -1$ to $x = 1$.
>
> $$A(x) = (1-x^2)^2$$
>
> $$V = \int_{-1}^{1} (1-x^2)^2\,dx = 2\int_0^1 (1 - 2x^2 + x^4)\,dx$$ (even function)
> $$= 2\left[x - \frac{2x^3}{3} + \frac{x^5}{5}\right]_0^1 = 2\left(1 - \frac{2}{3} + \frac{1}{5}\right) = 2\cdot\frac{8}{15} = \frac{16}{15}$$

## Summary

| Method | When to Use | Formula |
|---|---|---|
| Disk | Revolve around axis, no gap | $\pi\int[R(x)]^2\,dx$ |
| Washer | Revolve with gap between curves | $\pi\int([R]^2 - [r]^2)\,dx$ |
| Cross-section | Known cross-section shape | $\int A(x)\,dx$ |

## Tips

> [!tip] Key Points
> - **Always sketch** the region and the axis of revolution.
> - **Identify outer and inner radius** carefully for washers. The radius is the **distance from the axis of revolution to the curve**.
> - **If revolving around a line other than an axis** (like $y = 3$), adjust the radius: distance from curve to line.
> - **Don't square the difference:** $\pi(R^2 - r^2) \neq \pi(R - r)^2$. Square each radius separately, then subtract.
> - **Disk vs washer:** disk is just a washer with $r = 0$.
> - Choose **$dx$ or $dy$** based on which makes the radius easier to express.
