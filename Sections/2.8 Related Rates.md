# 2.8 Related Rates

> [!info] Section 2.8 — Related Rates
> Covered on Feb 9. Stewart Calculus 9e, Section 2.8.

## Overview

In a related rates problem, several quantities change with time and are connected by an equation. We know some of the rates and want to find another. The strategy: write an equation relating the quantities, then **differentiate with respect to $t$** (implicit differentiation) to relate the rates.

See [[Related Rates Problem-Solving Strategy]] for the full step-by-step algorithm and formula sheet.

## General Strategy

1. Draw a diagram and assign variables to changing quantities
2. Write an equation relating the variables
3. Differentiate both sides with respect to $t$
4. Substitute known values and solve for the unknown rate

> [!tip] Critical Rule
> **Never substitute numbers before differentiating.** If a quantity changes with time, keep it as a variable until after you differentiate.

## Worked Examples

> [!example] Example 1 — Expanding Sphere (Volume)
> Air is pumped into a spherical balloon at $100$ cm³/s. How fast is the radius increasing when $r = 25$ cm?
>
> **Given:** $\frac{dV}{dt} = 100$. **Find:** $\frac{dr}{dt}$ when $r = 25$.
>
> **Equation:** $V = \frac{4}{3}\pi r^3$
>
> **Differentiate:** $\frac{dV}{dt} = 4\pi r^2 \frac{dr}{dt}$
>
> **Substitute:** $100 = 4\pi(25)^2\frac{dr}{dt} = 2500\pi\frac{dr}{dt}$
>
> $$\frac{dr}{dt} = \frac{100}{2500\pi} = \frac{1}{25\pi} \approx 0.013 \text{ cm/s}$$

> [!example] Example 2 — Sliding Ladder (Pythagorean Theorem)
> A 10-ft ladder leans against a wall. The bottom slides away at 1 ft/s. How fast is the top sliding down when the bottom is 6 ft from the wall?
>
> **Setup:** Let $x$ = base distance, $y$ = top height. $x^2 + y^2 = 100$.
>
> When $x = 6$: $y = \sqrt{100 - 36} = 8$.
>
> **Differentiate:** $2x\frac{dx}{dt} + 2y\frac{dy}{dt} = 0$
>
> **Substitute:** $2(6)(1) + 2(8)\frac{dy}{dt} = 0$
>
> $$\frac{dy}{dt} = -\frac{12}{16} = -\frac{3}{4} \text{ ft/s}$$
>
> The top slides down at $\frac{3}{4}$ ft/s. (Negative sign confirms downward motion.)

> [!example] Example 3 — Conical Tank (Similar Triangles)
> Water drains from an inverted cone (height 10 ft, top radius 4 ft) at 3 ft³/min. How fast is the water level dropping when depth is 5 ft?
>
> **Eliminate $r$:** By similar triangles, $\frac{r}{h} = \frac{4}{10}$, so $r = \frac{2h}{5}$.
>
> **Volume in terms of $h$ only:**
> $$V = \frac{1}{3}\pi r^2 h = \frac{1}{3}\pi\left(\frac{2h}{5}\right)^2 h = \frac{4\pi h^3}{75}$$
>
> **Differentiate:** $\frac{dV}{dt} = \frac{4\pi}{25}h^2\frac{dh}{dt}$
>
> **Substitute** ($\frac{dV}{dt} = -3$, $h = 5$):
> $$-3 = \frac{4\pi}{25}(25)\frac{dh}{dt} = 4\pi\frac{dh}{dt}$$
>
> $$\frac{dh}{dt} = -\frac{3}{4\pi} \approx -0.239 \text{ ft/min}$$

> [!example] Example 4 — Two Cars (Distance)
> Car A heads north at 60 mph, Car B heads east at 80 mph. Both start from the same intersection. How fast is the distance between them changing after 2 hours?
>
> After 2 hrs: $a = 120$ mi (north), $b = 160$ mi (east). Distance: $c = \sqrt{120^2 + 160^2} = 200$ mi.
>
> **Equation:** $a^2 + b^2 = c^2$
>
> **Differentiate:** $2a\frac{da}{dt} + 2b\frac{db}{dt} = 2c\frac{dc}{dt}$
>
> **Substitute:** $2(120)(60) + 2(160)(80) = 2(200)\frac{dc}{dt}$
>
> $14400 + 25600 = 400\frac{dc}{dt}$
>
> $$\frac{dc}{dt} = \frac{40000}{400} = 100 \text{ mph}$$

> [!example] Example 5 — Shadow Problem (Similar Triangles)
> A 6-ft person walks away from a 15-ft streetlight at 4 ft/s. How fast is the tip of their shadow moving?
>
> Let $x$ = person's distance from light, $s$ = shadow length.
>
> **Similar triangles:** $\frac{15}{x + s} = \frac{6}{s}$, so $15s = 6x + 6s$, giving $9s = 6x$, i.e., $s = \frac{2x}{3}$.
>
> **Tip of shadow** is at distance $x + s = x + \frac{2x}{3} = \frac{5x}{3}$ from the light.
>
> **Differentiate:** $\frac{d}{dt}(x + s) = \frac{5}{3}\frac{dx}{dt} = \frac{5}{3}(4) = \frac{20}{3}$ ft/s.
>
> The shadow tip moves at $\frac{20}{3} \approx 6.67$ ft/s (faster than the person).

> [!example] Example 6 — Expanding Rectangle
> The length of a rectangle increases at 3 cm/s and the width increases at 2 cm/s. How fast is the area increasing when $l = 10$ cm and $w = 6$ cm?
>
> $A = lw$. Differentiate (product rule): $\frac{dA}{dt} = \frac{dl}{dt} \cdot w + l \cdot \frac{dw}{dt}$
>
> $\frac{dA}{dt} = 3(6) + 10(2) = 18 + 20 = 38$ cm²/s.

## Common Mistakes

> [!tip] Watch Out For
> - **Substituting before differentiating** — the #1 error. If you plug in $r = 25$ before differentiating, you get $\frac{d}{dt}(25) = 0$, losing the rate.
> - **Forgetting to find intermediate values** — you often need to compute one variable before substituting (e.g., finding $y$ from $x^2 + y^2 = 100$ before substituting).
> - **Sign errors** — decreasing quantities have negative rates. "Drains at 3 ft³/min" means $\frac{dV}{dt} = -3$.
> - **Not eliminating variables** — if your equation has 3 variables but you only have 2 rates, you need a constraint (like similar triangles) to eliminate one.
