# 3.2 The Mean Value Theorem

> [!info] Section 3.2 — The Mean Value Theorem
> Covered on Feb 23. Stewart Calculus 9e, Section 3.2.

## Overview

The Mean Value Theorem (MVT) connects the average rate of change of a function to its instantaneous rate of change. It is the theoretical backbone behind many results in calculus, including why the sign of $f'$ determines increasing/decreasing behavior.

See [[Mean Value Theorem]] for proofs, consequences, and applications.

## Rolle's Theorem

> [!thm] Rolle's Theorem
> If $f$ is continuous on $[a, b]$, differentiable on $(a, b)$, and $f(a) = f(b)$, then there exists $c \in (a, b)$ with $f'(c) = 0$.

Rolle's is a special case of MVT (when the secant line is horizontal).

> [!example] Example 1 — Rolle's Theorem
> Show that $f(x) = x^3 - 3x + 1$ has a horizontal tangent in $(0, \sqrt{3})$.
>
> $f(0) = 1$, $f(\sqrt{3}) = 3\sqrt{3} - 3\sqrt{3} + 1 = 1$. So $f(0) = f(\sqrt{3})$.
>
> Continuous and differentiable. By Rolle's, $\exists\, c$ with $f'(c) = 3c^2 - 3 = 0$, so $c = 1 \in (0, \sqrt{3})$. ✓

## The Mean Value Theorem

> [!thm] Mean Value Theorem
> If $f$ is continuous on $[a, b]$ and differentiable on $(a, b)$, then there exists $c \in (a, b)$ such that
> $$f'(c) = \frac{f(b) - f(a)}{b - a}$$

> [!example] Example 2 — Find the $c$
> Verify MVT for $f(x) = x^3 - x$ on $[0, 2]$.
>
> $\frac{f(2) - f(0)}{2 - 0} = \frac{6 - 0}{2} = 3$
>
> Find $c$: $f'(c) = 3c^2 - 1 = 3 \implies c^2 = \frac{4}{3} \implies c = \frac{2}{\sqrt{3}} \approx 1.155 \in (0, 2)$ ✓

> [!example] Example 3 — Speed Trap
> A car passes a toll booth at 1:00 PM and another toll booth 80 miles away at 2:00 PM. Show the car was traveling exactly 80 mph at some moment.
>
> Let $s(t)$ = position. $s(1) = 0$, $s(2) = 80$. By MVT:
> $$s'(c) = \frac{80 - 0}{2 - 1} = 80 \text{ mph}$$
> for some $c \in (1, 2)$.

## Increasing/Decreasing Test

The most important consequence of MVT:

> [!thm] Increasing/Decreasing Test
> On an interval $I$:
> - If $f'(x) > 0$ for all $x \in I$, then $f$ is **increasing** on $I$
> - If $f'(x) < 0$ for all $x \in I$, then $f$ is **decreasing** on $I$

> [!example] Example 4 — Increasing/Decreasing Intervals
> $f(x) = x^3 - 12x + 1$
>
> $f'(x) = 3x^2 - 12 = 3(x-2)(x+2)$
>
> | Interval | Sign of $f'$ | Behavior |
> |---|---|---|
> | $(-\infty, -2)$ | $+$ | Increasing |
> | $(-2, 2)$ | $-$ | Decreasing |
> | $(2, \infty)$ | $+$ | Increasing |

> [!example] Example 5 — Proving Uniqueness of a Root
> Show $f(x) = x^3 + 3x + 1$ has exactly one real root.
>
> $f'(x) = 3x^2 + 3 > 0$ for all $x$. So $f$ is strictly increasing on $\mathbb{R}$.
>
> A strictly increasing function crosses the $x$-axis **at most once**.
>
> $f(-1) = -3 < 0$, $f(0) = 1 > 0$. By IVT, at least one root exists in $(-1, 0)$.
>
> Combining: exactly one root. $\blacksquare$

## Other Consequences

> [!thm] Functions with Zero Derivative Are Constant
> If $f'(x) = 0$ for all $x$ in $(a,b)$, then $f(x) = C$ (constant) on $(a,b)$.

> [!thm] Functions with Equal Derivatives Differ by a Constant
> If $f'(x) = g'(x)$ for all $x$ in $(a,b)$, then $f(x) = g(x) + C$.

This is why antiderivatives always include "$+ C$".

## Tips

> [!tip] Key Points
> - MVT requires **both** continuity on $[a,b]$ and differentiability on $(a,b)$. If either fails, the conclusion may not hold.
> - MVT guarantees existence of $c$ but doesn't give a formula to find it (except in simple cases).
> - The **Increasing/Decreasing Test** is the result you'll use most from this section.
> - MVT is the theoretical justification — in practice, you use the sign of $f'$ directly.
