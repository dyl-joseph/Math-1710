# 4.1 The Area Under a Curve

> [!info] Section 4.1 â€” Areas and Distances
> Covered on Mar 31 and Apr 2. Stewart Calculus 9e, Section 4.1.

## Overview

The area problem is the second great problem of calculus (the first was tangent lines/derivatives). We approximate the area under a curve using rectangles, then take a limit to get the exact area.

See [[Sigma Notation and Riemann Sums]] for summation formulas and Riemann sum details.

## The Area Problem

**Goal:** Find the area $A$ of the region bounded by $y = f(x)$, $y = 0$, $x = a$, and $x = b$, where $f(x) \ge 0$.

**Approach:** Approximate with $n$ rectangles, then let $n \to \infty$.

## Setting Up the Sum

Divide $[a, b]$ into $n$ equal subintervals: $\Delta x = \frac{b - a}{n}$

Endpoints: $x_0 = a,\ x_1 = a + \Delta x,\ \ldots,\ x_n = b$

> [!def] Area as a Limit
> $$A = \lim_{n \to \infty} \sum_{i=1}^{n} f(x_i^*)\Delta x$$
> where $x_i^*$ is any sample point in $[x_{i-1}, x_i]$.

For continuous $f \ge 0$, this limit exists and gives the same value regardless of the choice of sample points.

## Worked Examples

> [!example] Example 1 â€” Approximate $\int_0^2 x^2\,dx$ with $n = 4$
> $\Delta x = \frac{2}{4} = 0.5$
>
> **Right endpoints:** $0.5, 1, 1.5, 2$
> $$R_4 = 0.5[0.25 + 1 + 2.25 + 4] = 0.5(7.5) = 3.75$$
>
> **Left endpoints:** $0, 0.5, 1, 1.5$
> $$L_4 = 0.5[0 + 0.25 + 1 + 2.25] = 0.5(3.5) = 1.75$$
>
> Average: $\frac{3.75 + 1.75}{2} = 2.75$. Exact: $\frac{8}{3} \approx 2.667$.

> [!example] Example 2 â€” Exact Area of $f(x) = x^3$ on $[0, 1]$
> Right endpoints: $x_i = \frac{i}{n}$, $\Delta x = \frac{1}{n}$.
>
> $$\sum_{i=1}^{n} \left(\frac{i}{n}\right)^3 \cdot \frac{1}{n} = \frac{1}{n^4}\sum_{i=1}^{n} i^3 = \frac{1}{n^4} \cdot \frac{n^2(n+1)^2}{4} = \frac{(n+1)^2}{4n^2}$$
>
> $$A = \lim_{n \to \infty} \frac{(n+1)^2}{4n^2} = \frac{1}{4}$$

> [!example] Example 3 â€” Exact Area of $f(x) = 3x + 1$ on $[0, 2]$
> $x_i = \frac{2i}{n}$, $\Delta x = \frac{2}{n}$.
>
> $$\sum_{i=1}^{n} f\left(\frac{2i}{n}\right) \cdot \frac{2}{n} = \frac{2}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n}\left(\frac{6i}{n} + 1\right) = \frac{2}{n}\left(\frac{6}{n}\cdot\frac{n(n+1)}{2} + n\right)$$
> $$= \frac{2}{n}\left(3(n+1) + n\right) = \frac{2(4n+3)}{n} = 8 + \frac{6}{n}$$
>
> $A = \lim_{n \to \infty}\left(8 + \frac{6}{n}\right) = 8$
>
> Check: this is a trapezoid with parallel sides $f(0) = 1$ and $f(2) = 7$, width $2$.
> Area $= \frac{1}{2}(1 + 7)(2) = 8$. âś“

## Distance from Velocity

If $v(t) \ge 0$ is velocity, then the distance traveled from $t = a$ to $t = b$ is:

$$d = \lim_{n \to \infty} \sum_{i=1}^{n} v(t_i^*)\Delta t$$

This is the same area-under-the-curve idea applied to velocity.

> [!example] Example 4 â€” Distance Estimation
> A car's velocity readings every 2 seconds:
>
> | $t$ (s) | 0 | 2 | 4 | 6 | 8 | 10 |
> |---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
> | $v$ (ft/s) | 0 | 10 | 18 | 24 | 28 | 30 |
>
> Right-endpoint estimate ($\Delta t = 2$):
> $d \approx 2(10 + 18 + 24 + 28 + 30) = 2(110) = 220$ ft
>
> Left-endpoint estimate:
> $d \approx 2(0 + 10 + 18 + 24 + 28) = 2(80) = 160$ ft
>
> Best estimate: average $= 190$ ft.

## Tips

> [!tip] Key Points
> - **Overestimate vs underestimate:** For $f$ increasing, right sums overestimate and left sums underestimate. Reversed for decreasing $f$.
> - **The summation formulas** ($\sum i$, $\sum i^2$, $\sum i^3$) are essential for computing exact areas from limits.
> - **Factor out constants** from the sum before applying formulas.
> - **The limit step:** after substituting the summation formulas, divide everything by the highest power of $n$ (same technique as limits at infinity).
> - This section sets up the machinery â€” the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus (Section 4.3) will provide a much faster way to compute areas.
