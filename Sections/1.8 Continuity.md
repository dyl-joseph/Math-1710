# 1.8 Continuity

Intuitively, a function is **continuous** if you can draw its graph without lifting your pen. There are no breaks, holes, or jumps. This section makes that idea precise using limits and then develops powerful consequences — especially the [[Intermediate Value Theorem]].

---

## Definition of Continuity at a Point

> [!def] Continuity at a Point
> A function $f$ is **continuous at** $x = a$ if the following three conditions hold:
> 1. $f(a)$ is defined.
> 2. $\lim_{x\to a} f(x)$ exists.
> 3. $\lim_{x\to a} f(x) = f(a)$.
>
> If any one of these fails, $f$ is **discontinuous** at $a$.

In one equation: continuity at $a$ means
$$\lim_{x\to a} f(x) = f(a)$$
which implicitly requires both sides to exist.

---

## One-Sided Continuity

> [!def] Continuity from the Left
> $f$ is **continuous from the left** at $a$ if
> $$\lim_{x\to a^-} f(x) = f(a).$$

> [!def] Continuity from the Right
> $f$ is **continuous from the right** at $a$ if
> $$\lim_{x\to a^+} f(x) = f(a).$$

A function is continuous at $a$ if and only if it is continuous from both the left and the right at $a$.

---

## Continuity on an Interval

> [!def] Continuity on a Closed Interval
> A function $f$ is **continuous on $[a,b]$** if:
> 1. $f$ is continuous at every point in the open interval $(a,b)$,
> 2. $f$ is continuous from the right at $a$, and
> 3. $f$ is continuous from the left at $b$.

This is the version needed for the [[Intermediate Value Theorem]] — we only require one-sided continuity at the endpoints.

A function is **continuous on an open interval** $(a,b)$ if it is continuous at every point of that interval.

---

## Types of Discontinuity

When $f$ is discontinuous at $a$, the discontinuity is classified by what goes wrong. See [[Types of Discontinuity]] for full details and examples.

| Type | What Happens |
|------|-------------|
| **Removable** | $\lim_{x\to a} f(x)$ exists but $\neq f(a)$ (or $f(a)$ undefined). A "hole" in the graph. |
| **Jump** | Both one-sided limits exist but $\lim_{x\to a^-} f(x) \neq \lim_{x\to a^+} f(x)$. The graph "jumps." |
| **Infinite** | At least one one-sided limit is $\pm\infty$. Vertical asymptote. |

## Continuous Functions — The Standard Library

> [!thm] Continuity of Elementary Functions
> The following functions are **continuous on their domains**:
> - **Polynomials:** continuous on $(-\infty, \infty)$.
> - **Rational functions** $\frac{p(x)}{q(x)}$: continuous wherever $q(x) \neq 0$.
> - **Root functions** $\sqrt[n]{x}$: continuous on their domain (all reals if $n$ is odd; $[0,\infty)$ if $n$ is even).
> - **Trigonometric functions:** $\sin x$ and $\cos x$ are continuous on $(-\infty,\infty)$. The others ($\tan, \cot, \sec, \csc$) are continuous wherever they are defined.
> - **Exponential functions** $a^x$: continuous on $(-\infty, \infty)$.
> - **Logarithmic functions** $\log_a x$: continuous on $(0, \infty)$.

> [!tip] Most Functions Are Continuous on Their Domain
> Nearly every function you encounter in Calc I is continuous at every point where it is defined. Discontinuities typically arise at points **outside** the natural domain (like $x = 0$ for $1/x$) or at the seams of a piecewise definition.

## Building Continuous Functions

> [!thm] Arithmetic of Continuous Functions
> If $f$ and $g$ are continuous at $a$ and $c$ is a constant, then the following are also continuous at $a$:
> $$f + g, \quad f - g, \quad cf, \quad fg, \quad \frac{f}{g} \text{ (provided } g(a) \neq 0\text{)}$$

> [!thm] Composite Function Theorem
> If $g$ is continuous at $a$ and $f$ is continuous at $g(a)$, then the composite function $f \circ g$ is **continuous at $a$**:
> $$\lim_{x\to a} f(g(x)) = f\!\left(\lim_{x\to a} g(x)\right) = f(g(a)).$$

This theorem is extremely useful. It says we can "pass the limit inside" a continuous outer function:

$$\lim_{x\to a} f(g(x)) = f\!\left(\lim_{x\to a} g(x)\right)$$

provided $f$ is continuous at the value $\lim_{x\to a} g(x)$.

> [!example] Applying the Composite Theorem
> $$\lim_{x\to 0} \sin(x^2 + 1) = \sin\!\left(\lim_{x\to 0}(x^2+1)\right) = \sin(1)$$
>
> This works because $\sin$ is continuous everywhere and $\lim_{x\to 0}(x^2+1) = 1$.

## The Intermediate Value Theorem

> [!thm] Intermediate Value Theorem (IVT)
> If $f$ is continuous on $[a,b]$ and $N$ is any number between $f(a)$ and $f(b)$ (where $f(a) \neq f(b)$), then there exists at least one $c \in (a,b)$ such that $f(c) = N$.

**Geometric meaning:** A continuous curve from $(a, f(a))$ to $(b, f(b))$ must hit every $y$-value in between.

**Root-finding corollary:** If $f$ is continuous on $[a,b]$ and $f(a)$ and $f(b)$ have opposite signs, there is a root $c \in (a,b)$ with $f(c) = 0$.

See [[Intermediate Value Theorem]] for the full treatment, proof idea, bisection method, and additional examples.

## Worked Examples

> [!example] Example 1 — Classify discontinuities of $f(x) = \dfrac{x^2-1}{x-1}$
> The function is undefined at $x = 1$ (denominator is zero), so there is a discontinuity there. Factor:
> $$f(x) = \frac{(x-1)(x+1)}{x-1} = x+1, \quad x \neq 1$$
>
> So $\lim_{x\to 1} f(x) = 2$, but $f(1)$ is undefined.
>
> The limit exists but the function value does not: **removable discontinuity** at $x = 1$.
>
> The function is continuous at every other real number (it equals the polynomial $x + 1$ away from $x = 1$).

> [!example] Example 2 — Find $c$ so a piecewise function is continuous
> $$f(x) = \begin{cases} x^2 + c & x < 2 \\ 3x - 1 & x \geq 2 \end{cases}$$
>
> For $f$ to be continuous at $x = 2$, we need:
> $$\lim_{x\to 2^-} f(x) = \lim_{x\to 2^+} f(x) = f(2)$$
>
> **Right side and function value:** $f(2) = 3(2) - 1 = 5$ and $\lim_{x\to 2^+} f(x) = 5$.
>
> **Left side:** $\lim_{x\to 2^-} f(x) = (2)^2 + c = 4 + c$.
>
> Set them equal: $4 + c = 5$, so $c = 1$.

> [!example] Example 3 — Use IVT to show $x^3 + x - 1 = 0$ has a root in $(0,1)$
> Let $f(x) = x^3 + x - 1$. This is a polynomial, so it is continuous on $[0,1]$.
>
> $$f(0) = 0 + 0 - 1 = -1 < 0$$
> $$f(1) = 1 + 1 - 1 = 1 > 0$$
>
> Since $f$ is continuous on $[0,1]$ and $f(0) < 0 < f(1)$, the Intermediate Value Theorem guarantees there exists at least one $c \in (0,1)$ with $f(c) = 0$.

> [!example] Example 4 — Continuity of a composite function
> Where is $f(x) = \sqrt{4 - x^2}$ continuous?
>
> Write $f = h \circ g$ where $g(x) = 4 - x^2$ and $h(t) = \sqrt{t}$.
>
> - $g$ is a polynomial, continuous everywhere.
> - $h(t) = \sqrt{t}$ is continuous on $[0,\infty)$.
> - By the Composite Function Theorem, $f$ is continuous wherever $g(x) \geq 0$, i.e., $4 - x^2 \geq 0$, i.e., $-2 \leq x \leq 2$.
>
> So $f$ is continuous on $[-2, 2]$. (At the endpoints, continuity is one-sided.)

## Tips for Exams

> [!tip] Key Takeaways
> - **"Continuous on its domain"** is the default for standard functions. When asked "where is $f$ continuous?", the answer is usually "everywhere it is defined."
> - **Piecewise functions** are where continuity questions get interesting. Check the boundary points by computing both one-sided limits and comparing to $f(a)$.
> - **IVT guarantees existence, not uniqueness.** It tells you a root (or value) exists somewhere in an interval. It does not tell you how many there are or what the exact value is.
> - **To apply IVT, you must state three things:** (1) $f$ is continuous on $[a,b]$ (and why), (2) $f(a)$ and $f(b)$ have been computed, (3) $N$ is between them.
