# 2.5 The Chain Rule

> [!info] Section 2.5 — The Chain Rule
> Covered on Feb 2. Stewart Calculus 9e, Section 2.5.

## Overview

The chain rule tells us how to differentiate **composite functions** — functions of the form $f(g(x))$. It is arguably the most important differentiation rule, since most functions we encounter are compositions.

## The Chain Rule

> [!thm] The Chain Rule
> If $g$ is differentiable at $x$ and $f$ is differentiable at $g(x)$, then
> $$\frac{d}{dx}[f(g(x))] = f'(g(x)) \cdot g'(x)$$

In Leibniz notation: if $y = f(u)$ and $u = g(x)$, then

$$\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{dy}{du} \cdot \frac{du}{dx}$$

See [[Chain Rule]] for the proof and deeper treatment.

## The Outside-Inside Method

Think of $f(g(x))$ as "outer function applied to inner function":

1. **Differentiate the outer function**, leaving the inner function unchanged
2. **Multiply by the derivative of the inner function**

$$\underbrace{f'(g(x))}_{\text{derivative of outer, evaluated at inner}} \cdot \underbrace{g'(x)}_{\text{derivative of inner}}$$

## The General Power Rule

Combining the chain rule with the power rule:

> [!thm] General Power Rule
> $$\frac{d}{dx}[g(x)]^n = n[g(x)]^{n-1} \cdot g'(x)$$

This handles any "something to a power" where "something" is not just $x$.

## Chain Rule with Trig Functions

$$\frac{d}{dx}\sin(g(x)) = \cos(g(x)) \cdot g'(x)$$
$$\frac{d}{dx}\cos(g(x)) = -\sin(g(x)) \cdot g'(x)$$
$$\frac{d}{dx}\tan(g(x)) = \sec^2(g(x)) \cdot g'(x)$$

The same pattern applies to $\sec$, $\csc$, $\cot$ — just apply their usual derivatives and multiply by the inner derivative.

## Chain Rule with Exponentials

$$\frac{d}{dx}e^{g(x)} = e^{g(x)} \cdot g'(x)$$

The exponential function reproduces itself, but the chain rule contributes the factor $g'(x)$.

## Worked Examples

> [!example] Example 1 — General Power Rule
> Find $\frac{d}{dx}(2x+1)^5$.
>
> Outer: $u^5$, inner: $u = 2x+1$.
> $$5(2x+1)^4 \cdot 2 = 10(2x+1)^4$$

> [!example] Example 2 — Trig + Chain Rule
> Find $\frac{d}{dx}\sin(x^2)$.
>
> Outer: $\sin u$, inner: $u = x^2$.
> $$\cos(x^2) \cdot 2x = 2x\cos(x^2)$$

> [!example] Example 3 — Exponential + Chain Rule
> Find $\frac{d}{dx}e^{3x^2}$.
>
> Outer: $e^u$, inner: $u = 3x^2$.
> $$e^{3x^2} \cdot 6x = 6xe^{3x^2}$$

> [!example] Example 4 — Rewrite then Chain Rule
> Find $\frac{d}{dx}\sqrt{x^2+1}$.
>
> Rewrite as $(x^2+1)^{1/2}$. Outer: $u^{1/2}$, inner: $u = x^2 + 1$.
> $$\frac{1}{2}(x^2+1)^{-1/2} \cdot 2x = \frac{x}{\sqrt{x^2+1}}$$

> [!example] Example 5 — Double Chain Rule (Power of Trig)
> Find $\frac{d}{dx}\cos^3(x)$.
>
> This means $[\cos(x)]^3$. Outer: $u^3$, inner: $u = \cos x$.
> $$3\cos^2(x) \cdot (-\sin x) = -3\cos^2(x)\sin(x)$$

> [!example] Example 6 — Triple Composition
> Find $\frac{d}{dx}\sin(\cos(\tan x))$.
>
> Three layers — work from outside in:
> $$\cos(\cos(\tan x)) \cdot (-\sin(\tan x)) \cdot \sec^2(x)$$
> $$= -\cos(\cos(\tan x)) \cdot \sin(\tan x) \cdot \sec^2(x)$$

> [!example] Example 7 — Product Rule + Chain Rule
> Find $\frac{d}{dx}[x^2 \sin(3x)]$.
>
> Product rule first, chain rule on $\sin(3x)$:
> $$2x\sin(3x) + x^2 \cdot \cos(3x) \cdot 3 = 2x\sin(3x) + 3x^2\cos(3x)$$

> [!example] Example 8 — Quotient Rule + Chain Rule
> Find $\frac{d}{dx}\left[\frac{e^{2x}}{x^2+1}\right]$.
>
> Quotient rule, with chain rule on $e^{2x}$:
> $$\frac{e^{2x} \cdot 2 \cdot (x^2+1) - e^{2x} \cdot 2x}{(x^2+1)^2} = \frac{2e^{2x}(x^2 - x + 1)}{(x^2+1)^2}$$

## Tips

> [!tip] Key Reminders
> - **Always identify the outermost function first.** Ask: "What is the last operation?"
> - **Don't forget the inner derivative.** The most common chain rule error is writing $\frac{d}{dx}\sin(x^2) = \cos(x^2)$ instead of $2x\cos(x^2)$.
> - **Rewrite before differentiating:** Convert $\sqrt{\cdot}$ to $(\cdot)^{1/2}$, $\frac{1}{\cdot}$ to $(\cdot)^{-1}$, etc.
> - **When in doubt, label $u$:** Write $u = (\text{inner})$ explicitly, find $\frac{dy}{du}$ and $\frac{du}{dx}$, then multiply.
> - **Don't use chain rule when it's not needed:** $\frac{d}{dx}x^5$ is just power rule — no composition involved.
