# 1.7 Precise Definition of a Limit

## Why Do We Need This?

In Section 1.5, we described limits informally: "$f(x)$ gets closer and closer to $L$ as $x$ gets closer and closer to $a$." This intuition is useful, but it is **too vague** for rigorous mathematics:

- What does "closer and closer" actually mean?
- How close is close enough?
- How can we *prove* that a limit equals a specific value?

The $\varepsilon$-$\delta$ definition, formalized by Karl Weierstrass in the 19th century, answers all of these questions with a precise, quantified statement.

---

## The Epsilon-Delta Definition

> [!def] Precise Definition of a Limit
> Let $f$ be a function defined on some open interval containing $a$, except possibly at $a$ itself. We say
> $$\lim_{x \to a} f(x) = L$$
> if **for every** $\varepsilon > 0$ **there exists** a $\delta > 0$ such that
> $$0 < |x - a| < \delta \implies |f(x) - L| < \varepsilon.$$

For a thorough breakdown of every piece of this definition, see [[Epsilon-Delta Definition]].

---

## Geometric Interpretation

The definition has a clean visual meaning:

- **$\varepsilon$ controls the output:** The condition $|f(x) - L| < \varepsilon$ restricts $f(x)$ to a **horizontal strip** of total width $2\varepsilon$ centered at $y = L$ (from $y = L - \varepsilon$ to $y = L + \varepsilon$).
- **$\delta$ controls the input:** The condition $|x - a| < \delta$ restricts $x$ to a **vertical strip** of total width $2\delta$ centered at $x = a$ (from $x = a - \delta$ to $x = a + \delta$).
- **The definition says:** For any horizontal strip (no matter how thin), there exists a vertical strip such that the curve $y = f(x)$ inside the vertical strip (minus the point $x = a$) stays entirely inside the horizontal strip.

---

## The Challenge-Response Game

A helpful metaphor: think of a two-player game.

1. **Challenger** picks $\varepsilon > 0$ — a tolerance for the output.
2. **Responder** must find $\delta > 0$ — a tolerance for the input.
3. **Check:** Whenever $0 < |x - a| < \delta$, is $|f(x) - L| < \varepsilon$?

If the Responder **always has a winning strategy** (a formula for $\delta$ in terms of $\varepsilon$), the limit equals $L$. If the Challenger can find even one $\varepsilon$ that stumps the Responder, the limit is not $L$.

---

## How to Write Epsilon-Delta Proofs

The general strategy has three steps:

1. **Start with $|f(x) - L|$** and manipulate algebraically until it looks like $(\text{something}) \cdot |x - a|$ or can be bounded by such an expression.
2. **Choose $\delta$** in terms of $\varepsilon$ so that the bound $< \varepsilon$.
3. **Write the formal proof** — present the argument cleanly, going forward from the hypothesis.

For a comprehensive strategy guide, see [[Epsilon-Delta Proof Strategies]].

> [!tip] The Two-Phase Approach
> Always do **scratch work** first (working backward from the goal), then write the **formal proof** (going forward from hypothesis to conclusion). These are separate — the scratch work is not part of the proof.

---

## Worked Proof 1: Linear Function

**Prove** $\lim_{x \to 3}(4x - 5) = 7$.

### Scratch Work

We need to show: for every $\varepsilon > 0$, there exists $\delta > 0$ such that
$$0 < |x - 3| < \delta \implies |(4x - 5) - 7| < \varepsilon.$$

Simplify $|f(x) - L|$:
$$|(4x - 5) - 7| = |4x - 12| = 4|x - 3|$$

We need $4|x - 3| < \varepsilon$, which means $|x - 3| < \frac{\varepsilon}{4}$.

So if we choose $\delta = \frac{\varepsilon}{4}$, we're done.

### Formal Proof

> [!example] Proof: $\lim_{x \to 3}(4x - 5) = 7$
> Let $\varepsilon > 0$ be given. Choose $\delta = \dfrac{\varepsilon}{4}$. Note $\delta > 0$ since $\varepsilon > 0$.
>
> Suppose $0 < |x - 3| < \delta$. Then:
> $$|(4x - 5) - 7| = |4x - 12| = 4|x - 3| < 4\delta = 4 \cdot \frac{\varepsilon}{4} = \varepsilon.$$
>
> Since $\varepsilon > 0$ was arbitrary, $\lim_{x \to 3}(4x - 5) = 7$. $\blacksquare$

> [!tip] Linear Functions
> For any $\lim_{x \to a}(mx + b) = ma + b$ with $m \neq 0$: always $\delta = \frac{\varepsilon}{|m|}$.

---

## Worked Proof 2: Quadratic Function

**Prove** $\lim_{x \to 2} x^2 = 4$.

### Scratch Work

We need: for every $\varepsilon > 0$, there exists $\delta > 0$ such that
$$0 < |x - 2| < \delta \implies |x^2 - 4| < \varepsilon.$$

Factor:
$$|x^2 - 4| = |x - 2| \cdot |x + 2|$$

Problem: $|x + 2|$ depends on $x$. We can't just "solve for $\delta$" because the expression involves both $|x - 2|$ and $|x + 2|$.

**Strategy: Bound $|x + 2|$ by restricting $\delta$.**

Assume $\delta \leq 1$. Then $|x - 2| < 1$, so $1 < x < 3$, so $3 < x + 2 < 5$, which gives:
$$|x + 2| < 5$$

Now:
$$|x^2 - 4| = |x - 2| \cdot |x + 2| < |x - 2| \cdot 5$$

We need $5|x - 2| < \varepsilon$, so $|x - 2| < \frac{\varepsilon}{5}$.

We need **both** $\delta \leq 1$ and $\delta \leq \frac{\varepsilon}{5}$, so choose:
$$\delta = \min\!\left(1,\; \frac{\varepsilon}{5}\right)$$

### Formal Proof

> [!example] Proof: $\lim_{x \to 2} x^2 = 4$
> Let $\varepsilon > 0$ be given. Choose $\delta = \min\!\left(1, \dfrac{\varepsilon}{5}\right)$. Note $\delta > 0$.
>
> Suppose $0 < |x - 2| < \delta$.
>
> Since $\delta \leq 1$, we have $|x - 2| < 1$, so $1 < x < 3$, hence $|x + 2| < 5$.
>
> Since $\delta \leq \frac{\varepsilon}{5}$, we have $|x - 2| < \frac{\varepsilon}{5}$.
>
> Therefore:
> $$|x^2 - 4| = |x - 2| \cdot |x + 2| < \frac{\varepsilon}{5} \cdot 5 = \varepsilon.$$
>
> Since $\varepsilon > 0$ was arbitrary, $\lim_{x \to 2} x^2 = 4$. $\blacksquare$

> [!tip] Quadratic/Polynomial Functions
> Factor out $|x - a|$, restrict $\delta \leq 1$ to bound the remaining factor, then take $\delta = \min(1, \varepsilon / \text{bound})$.

## Worked Proof 3: Square Root Function

**Prove** $\lim_{x \to 4}\sqrt{x} = 2$.

### Scratch Work

We need: for every $\varepsilon > 0$, there exists $\delta > 0$ such that
$$0 < |x - 4| < \delta \implies |\sqrt{x} - 2| < \varepsilon.$$

Direct approach doesn't factor nicely. **Strategy: Rationalize** by multiplying and dividing by the conjugate.

$$|\sqrt{x} - 2| = |\sqrt{x} - 2| \cdot \frac{|\sqrt{x} + 2|}{|\sqrt{x} + 2|} = \frac{|x - 4|}{|\sqrt{x} + 2|}$$

Now we need to bound $\frac{1}{\sqrt{x} + 2}$. Since $\sqrt{x} \geq 0$ for all $x \geq 0$:
$$\sqrt{x} + 2 \geq 0 + 2 = 2$$

So:
$$\frac{1}{\sqrt{x} + 2} \leq \frac{1}{2}$$

Therefore:
$$|\sqrt{x} - 2| = \frac{|x - 4|}{\sqrt{x} + 2} \leq \frac{|x - 4|}{2}$$

We need $\frac{|x - 4|}{2} < \varepsilon$, so $|x - 4| < 2\varepsilon$.

Choose $\delta = 2\varepsilon$.

(We should also ensure $x \geq 0$ so $\sqrt{x}$ is defined. If $\delta \leq 4$, then $x > 4 - \delta \geq 0$. Since we only need this for small $\varepsilon$, and for large $\varepsilon$ the proof works anyway, we can take $\delta = \min(4, 2\varepsilon)$ to be safe — but since $\sqrt{x} + 2 \geq 2$ only requires $x \geq 0$, and $x$ is near 4, this is automatically satisfied for small $\delta$. For a clean proof, $\delta = 2\varepsilon$ suffices with a note that $x$ is in the domain.)

### Formal Proof

> [!example] Proof: $\lim_{x \to 4}\sqrt{x} = 2$
> Let $\varepsilon > 0$ be given. Choose $\delta = 2\varepsilon$.
>
> Suppose $0 < |x - 4| < \delta$ and $x > 0$ (so $\sqrt{x}$ is defined). Then $\sqrt{x} + 2 \geq 2 > 0$, so:
> $$|\sqrt{x} - 2| = \frac{|(\sqrt{x} - 2)(\sqrt{x} + 2)|}{\sqrt{x} + 2} = \frac{|x - 4|}{\sqrt{x} + 2} \leq \frac{|x - 4|}{2} < \frac{2\varepsilon}{2} = \varepsilon.$$
>
> Since $\varepsilon > 0$ was arbitrary, $\lim_{x \to 4}\sqrt{x} = 2$. $\blacksquare$

> [!tip] Square Root Functions
> Multiply and divide by the conjugate $\sqrt{x} + \sqrt{a}$. The numerator becomes $|x - a|$. Then bound $\frac{1}{\sqrt{x} + \sqrt{a}} \leq \frac{1}{\sqrt{a}}$ (for $a > 0$). This gives $\delta = \varepsilon\sqrt{a}$.

## One-Sided Limit Definitions

> [!def] Left-Hand Limit
> $\displaystyle\lim_{x \to a^-} f(x) = L$ means: for every $\varepsilon > 0$, there exists $\delta > 0$ such that
> $$a - \delta < x < a \implies |f(x) - L| < \varepsilon.$$
> (Only consider $x$ to the **left** of $a$.)

> [!def] Right-Hand Limit
> $\displaystyle\lim_{x \to a^+} f(x) = L$ means: for every $\varepsilon > 0$, there exists $\delta > 0$ such that
> $$a < x < a + \delta \implies |f(x) - L| < \varepsilon.$$
> (Only consider $x$ to the **right** of $a$.)

> [!tip] Comparison with Two-Sided
> The only difference from the two-sided definition is the hypothesis:
> - Two-sided: $0 < |x - a| < \delta$ (both sides)
> - Left: $a - \delta < x < a$ (left side only)
> - Right: $a < x < a + \delta$ (right side only)
>
> Recall: $\lim_{x \to a} f(x) = L$ if and only if both one-sided limits equal $L$.

---

## Infinite Limit Definition

> [!def] Infinite Limit
> $\displaystyle\lim_{x \to a} f(x) = \infty$ means: for every $M > 0$, there exists $\delta > 0$ such that
> $$0 < |x - a| < \delta \implies f(x) > M.$$

Instead of trapping $f(x)$ within $\varepsilon$ of a finite $L$, we require $f(x)$ to exceed any given bound $M$. The "game" changes: the Challenger picks a height $M$ (instead of $\varepsilon$), and the Responder must find $\delta$ so that $f(x) > M$ whenever $x$ is within $\delta$ of $a$.

> [!def] Negative Infinite Limit
> $\displaystyle\lim_{x \to a} f(x) = -\infty$ means: for every $M > 0$, there exists $\delta > 0$ such that
> $$0 < |x - a| < \delta \implies f(x) < -M.$$

> [!example] Example: $\lim_{x \to 0} \frac{1}{x^2} = \infty$
> **Scratch work:** Need $\frac{1}{x^2} > M$, i.e., $x^2 < \frac{1}{M}$, i.e., $|x| < \frac{1}{\sqrt{M}}$. Choose $\delta = \frac{1}{\sqrt{M}}$.
>
> **Proof:** Let $M > 0$ be given. Choose $\delta = \frac{1}{\sqrt{M}}$.
>
> Suppose $0 < |x - 0| < \delta = \frac{1}{\sqrt{M}}$. Then $|x| < \frac{1}{\sqrt{M}}$, so $x^2 < \frac{1}{M}$, hence:
> $$\frac{1}{x^2} > M.$$
>
> Therefore $\lim_{x \to 0} \frac{1}{x^2} = \infty$. $\blacksquare$

---

## Tips and Summary

> [!tip] Quick Reference
> - **Linear** $f(x) = mx + b$: Choose $\delta = \frac{\varepsilon}{|m|}$.
> - **Quadratic/Polynomial**: Factor out $|x - a|$, restrict $\delta \leq 1$, bound remaining factors, choose $\delta = \min\!\left(1, \frac{\varepsilon}{\text{bound}}\right)$.
> - **Square root**: Rationalize via conjugate, bound $\frac{1}{\sqrt{x}+\sqrt{a}} \leq \frac{1}{\sqrt{a}}$, choose $\delta = \varepsilon\sqrt{a}$.
> - **Always**: Do scratch work first, then write the formal proof forward.
> - **The $\min$ trick**: When you need multiple restrictions on $\delta$, take the minimum of all of them.
